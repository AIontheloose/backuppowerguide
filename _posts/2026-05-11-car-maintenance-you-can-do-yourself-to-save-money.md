## The Essential car maintenance you can do yourself to save money Tasks You Can Tackle This Weekend

### Oil and Filter Changes
Engine oil is the lifeblood of your vehicle, but its degradation is inevitable under Australia’s demanding thermal conditions. Modern engines demand precise viscosity ratings to function correctly, yet many drivers mistakenly stick to older 10W‑30 grades that are often too thick for contemporary engines in hot climates. This mismatch reduces fuel efficiency, increases internal friction, and accelerates sludge formation. Instead, you should aim for a full synthetic blend that meets current manufacturer specifications and carries the appropriate API and ACEA ratings. 

> **Pro Tip:** Check your oil level at exactly 12 pm noon. The direct Australian sunlight makes the dipstick markings significantly easier to read, and you should always wipe the dipstick with a clean lint-free rag before re‑inserting it for an accurate baseline.

When performing your own oil change, the filter is just as critical as the fluid itself. Replacing the oil filter every 5,000 km (or every 6 months) ensures that fine metallic particles and combustion byproducts are captured before they can circulate and clog vital engine passages. Even if the drained oil appears relatively clear, a saturated filter will bypass debris, causing accelerated wear. For a reliable baseline product suited to Australian conditions, consider researching Mobil 1 5W‑30 Full Synthetic Motor Oil 10L, which provides consistent lubrication across extreme temperature swings.

### Tyre Pressure and Brake Inspections
Australian road conditions vary wildly, from smooth metropolitan freeways to gravel and rocky tracks that aggressively abrade rubber and metal. This reality demands more frequent tyre checks and brake pad inspections. Braking pads wear out on average every 30,000 km in Australia, but rough terrain and frequent stop-start city driving can halve that interval. 

Regulatory requirements also mean that all new vehicles sold since 2014 must be fitted with a Tyre Pressure Monitoring System (TPMS) under Australian Design Rules. If your warning light illuminates, do not dismiss it. Ignoring TPMS alerts leads to uneven tyre wear, reduced grip, and potentially catastrophic blow‑outs during long highway journeys. You should maintain tyre pressure at 2.2 bar (32 psi) for front tyres and 2.0 bar (29 psi) for rear tyres, in accordance with AS 1402 standards. Check these pressures once a month using a calibrated digital gauge, adjusting for load or towing requirements.

> **Pro Tip:** Always use a torque wrench for wheel lug nuts. Over‑tightening is a common DIY error that can strip threads or cause nuts to loosen during vibration-heavy rural drives. Torque most Australian passenger cars to 120 Nm (88 ft‑lb), but always defer to your manufacturer’s specific specification plate.

### Coolant Flushing and Battery Swaps
Australia’s climate extremes place immense stress on cooling systems. Southern regions regularly experience 50 °C summer heat, while northern territories push 45 °C. This thermal load degrades water‑based coolant (antifreeze) over time, leading to corrosion and overheating. Coolant must meet AS 1942‑2015 and should be replaced every 5 years or 100,000 km, whichever comes first. Flushing the system involves mixing 50 % antifreeze with 50 % distilled water, running the engine until the temperature plateaus, then draining and refilling. For a compatible, high-grade solution, look into Prestone AS1942 Compliant Antifreeze Coolant 5L, which is formulated to handle extreme thermal cycling without precipitating harmful deposits.

The battery is another component that rarely fails unexpectedly without warning. Swapping a dead battery yourself can save up to $50 compared with a professional replacement that bundles in disposal and labour fees. Before removing the old unit, ensure you note the terminal orientation and secure the hold-down bracket properly. A loose battery will vibrate until internal plates fracture. For a reliable replacement unit tested for Australian voltage stability, explore Denso 12V Maintenance Free Car Battery 65Ah, which offers consistent cold-cranking performance even in coastal humidity.

## Common DIY Mistakes to Avoid

Even with good intentions, well-meaning enthusiasts frequently undermine their own efforts. The most prevalent error is using the wrong oil viscosity. As noted, clinging to older 10W‑30 grades in modern engines reduces fuel efficiency and increases wear. Another critical blunder is skipping the oil filter entirely. Some DIYers replace the oil but leave the old filter, which immediately contaminates the fresh fluid and causes sludge buildup. 

Ignoring TPMS alerts is equally dangerous. Drivers often assume a slow leak is harmless, but underinflated tyres generate excessive heat, particularly on long outback runs. Additionally, over‑tightening wheel lug nuts with a breaker bar instead of a calibrated torque wrench is a frequent mistake. This practice damages threads, distorts brake rotors, and ironically increases the likelihood of nuts loosening during transit. Always follow a systematic, specification-driven approach rather than relying on brute force or guesswork.

| Mistake | Consequence | Corrective Action | Prevention Strategy |
|---------|-------------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Wrong oil viscosity | Reduced efficiency, accelerated wear | Drain, flush, refill with correct spec | Consult owner’s manual before purchase |
| Skipping oil filter | Sludge buildup, bearing damage | Always replace filter with every oil change | Keep filter in toolkit at all times |
| Ignoring TPMS | Uneven wear, blowout risk | Check pressures weekly in summer | Use backlit digital gauge monthly |
| Over-torquing lug nuts | Stripped threads, rotor warping | Re-torque to spec after 50 km | Use calibrated click-type wrench only |

> **Pro Tip:** Keep a dedicated maintenance logbook in your glovebox. Record dates, kilometre readings, fluid types, and part numbers. This documentation not only proves compliance with warranty terms but also helps you spot recurring issues before they become expensive repairs.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I legally perform my own oil change and still keep my warranty?**
Yes, absolutely. Australian consumer law, specifically the Competition and Consumer Act 2010, protects your right to maintain your vehicle with aftermarket parts or independent labour, provided the work is carried out competently and uses components that meet the manufacturer’s specifications. Dealers cannot void your warranty simply because you performed the service yourself, though they may deny a claim if they can prove your work directly caused the failure. Always keep detailed receipts, photos of your work, and a clear service log to demonstrate compliance if a warranty claim arises.

**How often should I really check my TPMS when travelling in extreme heat?**
During summer months or long highway journeys in regions exceeding 40 °C, you should check your tyre pressures weekly rather than monthly. Heat causes air expansion, which can push pressures beyond safe limits or trigger false warnings if the system is calibrated for cooler baseline conditions. Always measure when tyres are cold for accurate readings, and adjust downward by 0.1 bar if you are carrying heavy loads or towing through the Outback.

**Is it safe to flush the cooling system myself, or should I pay a professional?**
Flushing the cooling system is entirely manageable for DIYers who follow the correct procedure and use the right antifreeze ratio. The key is using a proper 50/50 mix of distilled water and AS 1942‑2015 compliant antifreeze, ensuring the radiator cap is replaced if it shows corrosion, and bleeding air pockets according to your manual. However, if your vehicle has a complex sealed cooling loop, a hybrid thermal management system, or requires specific manufacturer-approved coolant formulations, consult a qualified technician first to avoid damaging sensitive sensors or seals.

**What happens if I over‑tighten my lug nuts on gravel roads?**
Over‑tightening distorts the wheel hub and brake rotor, leading to vibration, premature bearing failure, and uneven brake pad wear. On unsealed roads, vibration from rocks and potholes will exploit these stress points, potentially causing nuts to loosen or threads to shear during transit. Always torque to specification using a calibrated wrench, and re-check the nuts after 50 km of initial driving to ensure they have settled properly without exceeding the manufacturer’s limits.
