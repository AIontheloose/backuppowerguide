## Safety and Certification: The Non-Negotiable Standards for 2026

In 2026, the Australian Competition and Consumer Commission (ACCC) maintains its iron grip on electrical safety, and the standards for heating products are stricter than ever. The ACCC's recent crackdown on substandard imports has left no room for ambiguity: all electric blankets sold in Australia must carry the **AS/NZS 3816:2014** safety label. This standard specifically governs the safety of electrically heated blankets and related accessories. Furthermore, the internal components must comply with **AS/NZS 60947‑4‑1:2017**, which covers the electrical safety of control devices. These are not mere suggestions; they are the legal minimum for retail sale.

Many consumers fall into the trap of importing blankets directly from the UK or US via third-party marketplaces. This is a dangerous gamble. Overseas models often operate on different voltages and lack the specific thermal cutoff mechanisms mandated by Australian standards. Using a non-compliant blanket not only poses a severe fire risk but can also void your home insurance coverage in the event of a fault. Always inspect the product tag and controller before purchase. If you cannot locate the AS/NZS certification mark, put it back.

> **Pro Tip:** Always calculate the wattage per square metre to ensure safety and efficiency. The recommended safe range is 1.5 to 2.5 W m⁻². For a standard 120 × 200 cm blanket (approximately 2.4 m²), the total wattage should fall between 3.6 W and 6 W. Exceeding this limit increases fire risk significantly and may void insurance coverage in the event of a fault.

Modern blankets utilise advanced low-voltage heating elements to maintain efficiency while reducing shock hazards. A typical 4 W blanket running for eight hours consumes just 0.032 kWh. At an average electricity rate of $0.14 AUD per kWh, this costs a mere $0.04 per night. Despite the low nightly cost, energy consumption adds up over the winter months. Therefore, look for models with auto-off timers ranging from 30 minutes to 4 hours. These timers not only save money but also prevent the blanket from heating unnecessarily, extending the lifespan of the internal wiring.

> **Pro Tip:** Never fold or tuck the edges of your electric blanket while it is in use, as this can trap heat and damage the internal heating elements. Always lay it flat and ensure the controller cord is accessible and not trapped under the mattress, which can cause it to overheat.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Are electric blankets safe to use in 2026?
Yes, electric blankets are safe to use in 2026 provided they comply with current Australian safety standards. You must ensure the blanket carries the AS/NZS 3816:2014 certification label, which indicates it has passed rigorous testing for fire and electrical safety. The ACCC continues to monitor the market, and reputable retailers will only stock compliant products. Avoid any blanket that lacks clear certification or appears to be an uncertified import, as these pose significant risks.

### How much does it cost to run an electric blanket per night?
Running an electric blanket is generally very cost-effective compared to heating an entire room. A typical blanket consuming 4 W of power will use approximately 0.032 kWh over an eight-hour period. At an average electricity rate of $0.14 AUD per kWh, this equates to roughly $0.04 per night. Even if you use the blanket for longer periods or on higher settings, the cost remains minimal, often less than $0.10 per night, making it one of the most efficient ways to stay warm during the Australian winter.

### Can I wash an electric blanket?
Most modern electric blankets are machine washable, but you must follow the manufacturer's instructions carefully. Typically, you should unplug the controller and detach it from the blanket before washing. Many models can be washed on a gentle cycle at 30°C, but you should never use fabric softener as it can damage the heating elements. Always check the care label for specific guidance, and ensure the blanket is completely dry before reconnecting the controller to avoid electrical hazards.

### What is the difference between AS/NZS 3816 and AS/NZS 60947‑4‑1?
AS/NZS 3816:2014 is the specific standard for the safety of electrically heated blankets and accessories, covering aspects like thermal protection and construction. AS/NZS 60947‑4‑1:2017, on the other hand, relates to the electrical safety of control devices, such as the thermostat and controller unit. A compliant electric blanket must meet both standards to ensure the blanket itself is safe and that the controls managing the heat function correctly without risk of electrical fault.
