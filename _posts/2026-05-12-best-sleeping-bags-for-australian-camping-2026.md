## What to Look For in a Quality Sleeping Bag
Navigating the technical jargon can be daunting, but understanding a few core criteria will save you from costly mistakes. First, assess your intended climate and activity level. Australian weather is notoriously volatile; a mild evening in the Blue Mountains can rapidly descend into a frosty dawn if a cold front sweeps in from the Southern Ocean. Consequently, mummy-shaped bags are generally preferred over rectangular ones because they eliminate dead air space around the body, drastically improving thermal efficiency. 

Fill material is the next critical decision. Australian campers must choose between down and synthetic insulation. Down, typically sourced from geese or ducks, offers superior loft and compressibility, making it ideal for backpacking where every gram counts. However, down loses its insulating properties when wet, so it demands careful handling and a reliable waterproof stuff sack. Synthetic insulation, meanwhile, retains warmth even when damp and dries quickly, making it the pragmatic choice for coastal camping, tropical Queensland, or unpredictable rainforests. Look for bags with a high fill power (700+) if opting for down, and ensure the synthetic fill is labelled as hydrophobic or moisture-wicking. 

The shell and liner fabrics dictate durability and comfort. A denier rating of 20D to 30D offers a smart balance of lightweight portability and tear resistance, while a DWR (durable water repellent) coating sheds light drizzle. Inside, a brushed taffeta liner reduces friction against your base layers and adds a plush feel. Finally, check the zipper configuration. A two-way zipper allows you to vent excess heat on warmer nights and convert the bag into a quilt, while a anti-snare baffle prevents fabric from getting caught in the teeth. For extended trips, consider bags with a removable liner that can be machine-washed, a feature increasingly standard at Kathmandu and Macpac outlets. Always verify that your chosen gear complies with local park regulations, particularly in fire-prone zones where synthetic bags are often mandated over down due to lower combustion risks.

> **Pro Tip:** Always air out your sleeping bag immediately after returning from a trip. Roll it loosely in a well-ventilated area for at least 24 hours before storing it in its stuff sack to prevent mould growth and preserve the loft of the insulation. Never compress high-fill-power down for longer than a few days, as it will permanently lose its thermal resilience.

## Frequently Asked Questions
**Q: Can I use a summer-rated sleeping bag in winter?**  
A: Not safely. Summer bags are typically rated for +5 °C to +10 °C comfort zones. In Australian winter conditions, especially above 1,000 metres elevation or in exposed coastal zones, overnight lows frequently drop below freezing. Using an under-rated bag increases the risk of nocturnal hypothermia and severely degrades sleep quality, which directly impacts safety and navigation performance the following day.

**Q: How do I properly store a down sleeping bag to maintain loft?**  
A: Always store down bags loosely in their large cotton stuff sacks or hanging hooks, never compressed. Long-term compression collapses the down clusters, permanently reducing thermal efficiency. If you must use a compression sack for transport, only keep it tightly rolled for a few days at a time, then allow the bag to fully expand and air out for at least 48 hours before storage.

**Q: Are synthetic sleeping bags worth the extra weight for Australian conditions?**  
A: Absolutely for high-humidity or wet-climate camping. While down offers a superior warmth-to-weight ratio, synthetic insulation retains over 80% of its insulating capability when damp. For Queensland monsoons, Tasmanian rainforests, or extended coastal trips where drying opportunities are limited, synthetic bags are the more reliable and lower-maintenance choice.

**Q: What does “fill power” actually mean, and why does it matter?**  
A: Fill power measures the volume (in cubic inches) that one ounce of down occupies. Higher numbers indicate greater loft and thermal efficiency. An 800+ fill power down bag will be significantly lighter and more compressible than a 600-fill equivalent while delivering the same warmth. However, higher fill power also correlates with higher cost and greater vulnerability to moisture damage, so match it to your dry-climate or ultralight needs.

**Q: How often should I wash my sleeping bag?**  
A: Only when visibly soiled or odorous, typically once or twice per season. Frequent washing degrades DWR coatings and can mat the insulation. Spot-clean stains with a damp cloth and mild soap, and air out the bag after every trip. When washing is necessary, use a front-loading machine with a technical gear detergent, rinse twice, and tumble dry on low with clean tennis balls to restore loft.
