## How to Remove Mould from Walls and Ceilings Safely in 2026

G'day, I'm Sarah Chen. If you're reading this with a sigh, staring at that fuzzy patch on your ceiling while the Queensland humidity hits 75% again, you're not alone. In 2026, we're seeing mould outbreaks spike earlier than ever, especially across high-humidity zones like Northern NSW and Queensland where indoor levels regularly top 70%. I know that sinking feeling of frustration; you've scrubbed, you've painted, and yet there it is, mocking you from the corner of the bedroom. But don't panic. I've been in the trenches of Australian homes for years, and I'm here to walk you through removing mould safely without blowing your budget or your lungs.

### Why Mould is Making a Comeback in 2026

Mould isn't just a cosmetic nuisance; it's a moisture management issue. As we've tracked through the wet season peaks (November to March), the combination of rising humidity and older housing stock has made retrofits the battleground for mould. What I've found is that many homeowners are caught off guard because they think a quick wipe-down will solve it. Spoiler alert: it won't. Mould roots penetrate porous surfaces like plaster and timber. If you don't treat it properly, it'll be back before you can say "Bunnings run."

Moreover, the **2026 Building Code** now requires mould-resistant paint on all new construction, and retrofits are expected to meet "Mould-Resistant Finish" criteria. While this helps new builds, it highlights a critical point: prevention and proper remediation are now non-negotiable for maintaining property value and health standards. Ignoring this isn't just about aesthetics; it's about protecting your family from respiratory irritants and structural decay.

### Safety First: Your Essential Gear List

Before you grab a sponge, let's talk kit. I know, nobody likes spending money on safety gear, but mould spores are tiny invaders. In my experience, skipping protective gear is the number one mistake homeowners make. You need to protect your skin and your lungs.

> **Pro Tip:** Never underestimate the importance of the respirator. In my experience, homeowners often skip this to save time or money, but mould spores are microscopic and can trigger severe respiratory reactions, especially if you have asthma or allergies. A standard dust mask won't filter spores effectively. You must wear a fitted **N95 respirator**. If you're tackling a larger job, remember that a single-use N95 should be replaced after 8 hours of continuous wear to maintain its efficacy. Also, grab a pack of disposable nitrile gloves; they protect your skin from harsh chemicals and spores without the tear risk of latex.

You'll also want a portable exhaust fan to manage airflow. Mould hates dry, moving air, and your home does too during a remediation job.

### Assessing the Damage: DIY vs. Professional Help

How big is the patch? If it's small, DIY is fine. If it's massive, you might need a pro.

*   **DIY Range:** Ideal for surface mould covering less than 10 square metres. This covers most bathroom walls, window sills, and small ceiling patches. If the mould is isolated and the surface is intact, you can tackle this with the workflow below.
*   **Professional Help Needed:** If the mould covers more than 10 square metres, if it's growing behind skirting boards or inside wall cavities, or if you suspect black mould (*Stachybotrys*) in large quantities, call a licensed remediation specialist. Also, if you have compromised immunity or severe respiratory conditions, let the pros handle it.

### Step-by-Step Remediation Workflow

Let's get practical. Here's the safe, budget-aware method I use and recommend.

#### Step 1: Prep and Containment
Isolate the room. Close doors and tape off vents to stop spores drifting to other parts of the house. Lay drop sheets on the floor. Turn off HVAC systems. Put on your gear: N95 mask, gloves, and old clothes you don't mind ruining.

#### Step 2: Dry Removal and Cleaning
*   **Dry Removal:** For loose mould on ceilings, gently vacuum the area with a HEPA-filter vacuum if available. Do not use a standard vacuum, which can blow spores back into the air.
*   **The Clean:** Mix your cleaner. For a budget option, a solution of one part white vinegar to one part water works wonders for non-porous surfaces. For porous plaster or stubborn roots, a commercial **mould remover** is often worth the spend to ensure the kill rate is effective. Apply the solution, let it sit for 10–15 minutes, and scrub gently with a stiff brush. Avoid saturating the wall, as excess moisture feeds more mould.

#### Step 3: Drying and Dehumidifying
This is the step most people skip, and it's why mould returns. Use your exhaust fan and place your **dehumidifier** in the room, running it on max for at least 24 to 48 hours. The surface must be bone dry before you move to sealing. Use a moisture meter if you have one; aim for readings below 12%.

#### Step 4: Sealing and Painting
Once dry, apply a mould-resistant primer. This seals any remaining spores and prevents bleed-through. After the primer cures, apply two coats of high-quality **mould-resistant paint**. In 2026, there are fantastic options that also offer heat-reflective properties, helping to keep walls cooler and drier.

### Prevention and After-Care: Keeping Mould at Bay

Remediation is only half the battle. To stop the comeback, you need to manage your home's environment.

1.  **Humidity Monitoring:** Invest in a cheap hygrometer. Keep indoor humidity between 30% and 50%. If it creeps above 60%, your dehumidifier needs to kick in.
2.  **Ventilation:** Run exhaust fans during and for 20 minutes after showers or cooking. Open windows when the outside air is drier than inside.
3.  **Routine Checks:** Inspect high-risk areas monthly. Catching a patch when it's the size of a coin saves you hours of scrubbing later.
4.  **Furniture Spacing:** Pull furniture 50mm away from external walls to allow airflow and prevent condensation build-up behind cupboards.

### FAQ: Your Mould Questions Answered

**Can I use bleach to remove mould from walls?**
While bleach is a common household staple, experts in 2026 advise caution when using it on porous surfaces like plasterboard, as it often sits on top rather than killing the root system, potentially leading to rapid regrowth. For effective remediation, a diluted vinegar solution or a commercial mould remover containing quaternary ammonium compounds is generally more reliable for penetrating deep into wall textures. If you do choose bleach, always mix it with cold water in a well-ventilated area and never combine it with ammonia or other cleaners, as this creates dangerous chlorine gas.

**How long does it take for mould to dry out after cleaning?**
Drying time depends heavily on your room's airflow and ambient humidity, but you should plan for at least 24 to 48 hours of active dehumidification to ensure the substrate is completely dry. If the wall feels damp to the touch or a moisture meter reads above 12%, do not apply primer, as painting over dampness will trap moisture and cause the mould to return within weeks. Using portable heaters alongside fans can speed up the process, but always prioritise thorough drying over rushing the timeline.

**Is it safe to live in the house while removing mould?**
It is generally safe to remain in the house provided you strictly isolate the work area and wear proper PPE, but those with respiratory conditions should relocate during the cleaning phase. If the mould is extensive or involves black mould, the airborne spore count can become hazardous even outside the immediate room, making temporary relocation the safer choice. Always ensure your exhaust fans are vented outside and seal off the work zone with plastic sheeting to protect the rest of your living space.

**How do I stop mould from coming back after treatment?**
To prevent recurrence, you must address the underlying moisture source, whether that's a leaky pipe, poor insulation, or high indoor humidity from daily activities. Implementing a routine of humidity monitoring, improving cross-ventilation, and using mould-resistant paints on all retrofits creates a durable barrier against future outbreaks. Regularly inspecting high-risk zones and maintaining your home's thermal envelope will ensure your hard work lasts and keeps your property healthy and valuable.
