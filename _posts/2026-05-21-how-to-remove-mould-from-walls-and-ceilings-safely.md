### Why Mould Is More Than Just an Aesthetic Issue

It’s easy to dismiss mould as a cosmetic nuisance, but the reality is far more pressing. According to the 2024 Australian Health Survey, 23% of asthma-related hospital visits were directly linked to prolonged exposure to indoor mould. Beyond the health implications, mould actively breaks down plaster and timber, leading to costly structural repairs down the line. More importantly, it creates a vicious cycle: mould feeds on moisture, so if you don’t address the source, the problem will inevitably return. Tackling it early is an act of self-care for your home and your family.

#### Step 1.1 – Ventilate the Room
Open all windows and doors, or position a box fan facing outward to pull spores out of the house. Good airflow is your first line of defence.

#### Step 1.2 – Protect Yourself & Your Belongings
- Wear gloves, safety goggles, and a long-sleeved shirt to prevent skin irritation.
- Cover nearby furniture and flooring with drop cloths or heavy-duty plastic sheeting.
- Keep pets and children out of the work zone until the area is completely dry.

### 2. Pinpoint the Moisture Source First

Removing the visible mould is only half the battle. If you don’t tackle the moisture problem, the spores will simply return. Start by checking for leaking pipes, cracked roof flashings, or condensation buildup around windows. In bathrooms and kitchens, ensure exhaust fans are actually venting outside and not just recirculating damp air. Grab a hygrometer from your local hardware store and monitor your indoor humidity; aim to keep it below 50%. If you’re unsure how to fix a structural leak, a quick call to a licensed plumber or builder is far cheaper than rewiring a water-damaged ceiling later.

### 4. Step-by-Step Mould Removal Process

#### Step 4.1 – Remove Loose Spores
Gently vacuum the affected area using a HEPA-filter attachment. Do not brush dry mould first, as this launches spores into the air. Dispose of the vacuum bag or empty the canister immediately into a sealed outdoor bin.

#### Step 4.2 – Apply Your Cleaning Solution
For most homes, a hydrogen peroxide mould killer is the safest, most budget-friendly choice. Spray it generously over the affected area until it’s damp but not dripping. Let it sit for 10–15 minutes to penetrate the spores. For stubborn stains, leave it for up to 30 minutes.

#### Step 4.3 – Scrub Gently
Use a soft-bristled brush or non-abrasive sponge to scrub in circular motions. For ceilings, use a long-reach extension pole or a stabilised step ladder to avoid neck strain and maintain balance.

#### Step 4.4 – Rinse & Dry Thoroughly
Wipe the area with clean water and a microfiber cloth. **Crucial step:** After rinsing, allow the wall or ceiling to dry completely to a moisture content below 30% RH before applying any primer or paint. Use a moisture meter to verify this; rushing the drying phase traps residual dampness and guarantees recurrence.

### 6. Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: Can I use baking soda to remove mould?**  
A1: Baking soda is excellent for absorbing ambient moisture and provides mild cleaning power, but it isn’t a true fungicide. It works best as a pre-cleaning step to dry out a surface before applying a hydrogen peroxide mould killer or commercial spray. For heavy growth, relying on baking soda alone will likely result in quick regrowth.

**Q2: How long does it take for mould to re‑appear after cleaning?**  
A2: If the underlying moisture source remains unresolved, mould can visibly return within 24 to 48 hours. The key to lasting results is fixing leaks, improving cross-ventilation, and maintaining indoor humidity below 50%. Consistent humidity control and proper drying are what truly break the cycle.

**Q3: Is it safe to repaint over mould‑treated walls?**  
A3: Yes, you can repaint once the surface is completely dry and sealed, but you must apply a dedicated mould-resistant primer first. This primer acts as a chemical barrier that prevents spores from re-emerging through the paint film. Using standard interior paint alone will allow the mould to bleed through within months.

**Q4: What if I find mould behind wallpaper?**  
A4: Carefully peel back the wallpaper to expose the underlying plaster or drywall. Treat the exposed surface with a safe mould cleaning solution, allow it to dry fully, and then decide whether to rehang wallpaper or switch to paint. If the wall substrate feels soft or crumbly, the plaster may need patching before you repaint.

### Bottom Line

Mould on walls and ceilings is far more than a cosmetic annoyance; it’s a health hazard and a structural threat that demands a calm, methodical response. In 2026, the most effective and budget-friendly approach centres on protecting yourself with proper PPE, eliminating the moisture source, and using a hydrogen peroxide mould killer for safe, non-toxic treatment. Once the surface is thoroughly dried and sealed with a mould-resistant acrylic primer, your home will look fresh and breathe healthier. I strongly recommend starting with a small test patch, keeping your humidity control routine consistent, and investing in a dehumidifier usage schedule if you live in a coastal or high-rainfall region. You’ve got this, and with a little patience and the right tools, you’ll reclaim your living spaces for years to come. Happy cleaning!
