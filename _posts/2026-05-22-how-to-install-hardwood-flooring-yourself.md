**Choosing Your Timber Species & Installation Method**
Your wood choice directly impacts both cost and finishing. Australian blackbutt and spotted gum are fantastic mid‑range options that hold up beautifully in our climate. If you prefer a lighter, modern look, European oak works wonderfully, while walnut offers rich, dark tones but comes at a premium. Equally important is deciding between a **nail‑down** or **floating** installation. Nail‑down requires a solid timber or plywood subfloor and offers the most traditional, rigid feel. Floating floors click together over underlayment and are ideal for concrete slabs or upstairs renovations where weight and noise are concerns. For most DIYers tackling a ground‑floor room, nail‑down delivers the best value and longevity. ### 3. Step‑by‑Step Installation

#### 3.1 Acclimatise Your Boards
Hardwood is living timber. It expands and contracts with humidity, so skipping acclimatisation is the fastest way to end up with buckling boards or wide gaps later. Unpack the timber and stack it flat in the room you’re working in for at least **48 hours**. Cover the stack with a breathable plastic sheet to prevent dust buildup, but leave the ends open for airflow. Keep the room at normal living temperature and humidity—don’t turn on heaters or fans directly on the stack.

#### 3.2 Prepare the Sub‑Floor
1. **Clear & Clean** – Remove all furniture, debris, and old adhesive. Sweep thoroughly.
2. **Check Level** – Use a laser level or a 2‑metre straight edge. Any dips or humps over 3 mm must be sanded down or filled with a self‑leveling compound. Uneven subfloors are the #1 cause of squeaks.
3. **Install Underlayment** – Roll out plywood or cork underlayment, overlapping seams by at least 5 cm. Secure with a staple gun or construction adhesive.
4. **Lay Acoustic Pad** – Place the rubber pad over the underlayment to dampen footfall noise and protect your timber from moisture.

#### 3.3 Cut and Fit the First Row
- **Mark a Reference Line** – Snap a chalk line parallel to your longest wall, 10 mm away from it (this accounts for your expansion gap).
- **Cut the First Board** – Measure the room’s width at multiple points. Saw the first board to fit, using a 2‑inch saw guide to keep cuts perfectly straight.
- **Lay the Row** – Place the board along the reference line, tongue-side facing the room. Use 10 mm spacers along the wall to maintain the expansion gap.

#### 3.4 Nail the Boards
- **Positioning** – Drive finish nails at a 45‑degree angle into the tongue of each board. Space nails every 400 mm to prevent movement.
- **Countersink** – Use a nail set to gently push each head below the surface. Over‑nailing (spaced further than 500 mm) leaves boards vulnerable to buckling.
- **Work Backwards** – Continue laying rows, nailing each board before moving to the next. Stagger end joints by at least 300 mm for structural stability.

#### 3.5 Handle Odd Corners & Door Frames
- **Measure Twice** – For tight corners or around pipes, use a template made from cardboard or thin plywood. Transfer the shape to your board and cut with a jigsaw.
- **Undercut Door Frames** – Place a piece of timber flooring against the door jamb, mark it, and use a pull‑saw to undercut the frame. This allows the floor to slide underneath for a seamless finish.

#### 3.6 Sanding & Finishing
1. **First Coat** – Apply a thin, even layer of oil or polyurethane with a foam roller. Work in the direction of the grain. Let it dry for 12 hours.
2. **Light Sanding** – Use a 240‑grit sanding block to smooth any raised grain or roller marks. Vacuum thoroughly.
3. **Second Coat** – Apply a final coat for durability and sheen. Allow 24 hours of curing before walking on it, and 72 hours before moving furniture back in.

> **Pro Tip:** Always wear a dust mask and safety goggles during sanding. Fine timber particles can irritate your lungs and eyes, and a good respirator costs less than a hospital visit.

### 4. Top 5 Pro Tips for a Flawless Finish

1. **Use a laser level for every row** – Even a 0.5 mm deviation becomes glaringly obvious once you’ve laid 20 boards.
2. **Stagger end joints strategically** – Avoid T-joints aligning vertically. A randomised or herringbone stagger adds strength and visual interest.
3. **Keep your nail gun perpendicular** – Tilting the gun during nailing creates weak points and makes countersinking impossible.
4. **Choose cork underlayment in humid zones** – If you’re near the coast or in a tropical climate, cork naturally resists mould and provides superior acoustic buffering.
5. **Test your finish in a hidden corner** – Always apply a small patch of your chosen oil or polyurethane on scrap timber to verify colour and drying time before committing to the whole room.

### 5. Common Mistakes & How to Fix Them

| Mistake | Why It Happens | Fix |
|---------|---------------|-----|
| Ignoring acclimatisation | Rush to finish before boards adjust to room humidity | Let boards sit 48 hrs in the installation room |
| Skipping underlayment | Trying to cut costs early | Install plywood or cork underlayment regardless of subfloor type |
| Over‑nailing boards | Using incorrect spacing or angling the gun | Maintain 400 mm spacing and keep the nail gun at 45° |
| Wrong finish application | Applying too much product or skipping sanding between coats | Use thin, even coats and lightly sand between each layer |
| Forgetting expansion gaps | Nailing boards flush against walls | Always leave a 10 mm gap and cover with skirting boards later |

### 6. Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: How long does the entire installation take for a 20 m² room?**
A: If you’re a confident DIYer, plan for 3 to 4 days. The first day covers subfloor prep and board acclimatisation, days two and three handle laying and nailing, and the final day is reserved for finishing and curing. Beginners often need 5 to 6 days because of the learning curve with tools and alignment. Always factor in drying time between finish coats, as rushing this step will compromise the floor’s durability.

**Q2: Can I install hardwood flooring over existing laminate or tile?**
A: You absolutely can, but you must prepare the surface correctly. Laminate requires a solid plywood underlayment to create a stable base, while tile needs a self‑leveling compound to eliminate height differences. Both surfaces must be completely flat, clean, and dry before you roll out your acoustic pad. Skipping this prep will lead to squeaks, uneven boards, and a shorter lifespan for your timber.

**Q3: What’s the best underlayment for Australian homes?**
A: Cork underlayment is generally the top choice for most Australian climates because it naturally resists mould, provides excellent acoustic buffering, and handles humidity swings well. If you’re on a tighter budget, rubber acoustic pads are a solid alternative, though they offer slightly less thermal insulation. Always check the manufacturer’s recommendations for your specific timber species, as some woods perform better with thicker layers.

**Q4: How do I prevent gaps from forming between boards after installation?**
A: Gaps usually appear when timber isn’t allowed to acclimatise properly or when indoor humidity drops drastically during winter. The best prevention is maintaining a consistent indoor humidity level between 45 % and 55 % year‑round using a humidifier or dehumidifier. During installation, always leave a 10 mm expansion gap along every wall and secure boards firmly with evenly spaced nails. If gaps do appear, they often close naturally when humidity rises, but persistent wide gaps may require professional filling.

### ❓ Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I need to acclimatize hardwood flooring in Australia's variable climate?**  
A: Absolutely. In 2026, with shifting weather patterns, acclimatization is non-negotiable. Leave planks in the installation room for at least 72 hours before laying. This prevents cupping or gapping as the wood adjusts to your home's specific humidity and temperature levels.

**Q: Which underlayment is better for a climate-first home: Cork or Rubber?**  
A: Both have merits. Cork is renewable, breathable, and offers excellent thermal insulation, making it a top choice for eco-conscious builds. Rubber provides superior sound dampening for multi-story homes. For a "Climate-First" approach, I often recommend cork for its carbon-sequestering properties, paired with low-VOC adhesives.

**Q: Can I install hardwood flooring over a concrete slab in a humid coastal region?**  
A: Yes, but moisture management is critical. Ensure a vapor barrier is installed beneath the underlayment. Consider using engineered hardwood rather than solid timber, as it's more dimensionally stable in the fluctuating humidity levels common in coastal Australia.

**Q: What maintenance does sustainable flooring require in 2026?**  
A: Modern eco-floorings are designed for longevity. Use pH-neutral cleaners, avoid excessive water, and maintain indoor humidity between 40–60%. Regular dusting prevents abrasive wear, extending the life of your floor and reducing the need for refinishing.
