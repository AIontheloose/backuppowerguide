## How to Service a Generator: Oil Change & Spark Plug Guide

The power flickered out at 3:14 PM on a Tuesday in January 2026. Outside my regional NSW property, the sky had turned a bruised purple, and the wind was howling at 65 km/h. Inside, the silence was deafening. The grid had finally buckled under peak summer demand, and with household electricity tariffs pushing past $0.85c/kWh, running the air conditioner on grid power was financially impossible. I hit the starter cord on my standby unit, only to hear a sickening *clunk* followed by dead silence. Thirty minutes later, a neighbour’s generator roared to life, and I realised my unit had been sitting on old, sludge-thick oil and a carbon-fouled spark plug. That was the day I stopped trusting marketing brochures and started treating generator maintenance like a critical infrastructure task.

I’m Marcus Webb, and over my years reviewing energy systems across Australia, I’ve learned that a generator is only as reliable as its last service. In 2026, with bushfire season encroaching on suburban fringes and hybrid solar setups becoming the norm, a silent backup generator isn’t just an inconvenience—it’s a liability. Today, we’re cutting through the fluff. I’ll walk you through servicing your generator’s oil and spark plug using real-world parts, current 2026 pricing, and practical advice tailored to the Australian climate.

### Why This Service Matters Now in 2026

Before we grab the tools, let’s talk about why this is non-negotiable right now. The energy landscape has shifted dramatically. First, **bushfire-ready power** is no longer optional for regional and peri-urban homeowners. During the 2025 bushfire season, I documented cases where generators failed due to lack of service, leaving families without power for days. Regular maintenance extends reliability when the grid goes down. Second, **solar backup integration** is now standard. Many Australians pair generators with solar arrays for hybrid systems. In these setups, the generator often kicks in after the batteries have discharged. If your oil is old, the increased viscosity can delay cranking, causing the starter motor to drag and fail to turn the engine over fast enough to establish a stable frequency. Third, **government grants and rebates** for off-grid systems are evolving. While direct cash grants have changed, maintaining a documented service history is often required to validate system performance for future upgrades or energy bill rebate eligibility. A logged service record is your insurance policy against bureaucratic red tape. Finally, with **high electricity tariffs**, efficiency matters. A fouled spark plug can increase fuel consumption by up to 15%. Over a summer of blackouts, that extra fuel cost erodes the savings you're trying to make by avoiding grid peaks.

### Safety & Preparation Checklist

Never skip the prep work. Emergency power systems demand respect, not haste.
* Disconnect the negative terminal on the battery or remove the spark plug wire to prevent accidental ignition.
* Wear nitrile gloves and safety glasses. Engine oil contains heavy metals and combustion byproducts.
* Keep a Class ABC fire extinguisher within arm’s reach.
* Use only UN-approved, sealable containers for old oil and fuel.
* Work in a well-ventilated area. Carbon monoxide is odourless and lethal.
* Dispose of used oil at your local council hazardous waste facility or licensed recycling centre. Never pour it down drains or into soil.

### Step 1: The Oil Change – Keeping the Engine Cool and Clean

The oil is the lifeblood of your generator. It lubricates moving parts, cools the engine, and cleans away contaminants. In our harsh Aussie climate, especially when storing a generator in a hot shed or garage, getting the oil right is non-negotiable.

**Choosing the Right Fluid**
For most 2026 portable and standby units, you need **5 litres of 10W-40 diesel engine oil**. You might wonder why "diesel" oil

...is recommended for generators that frequently run on gasoline or dual-fuel setups. The answer lies in the additive package. Diesel-specific oils contain higher concentrations of detergents and anti-wear compounds like zinc and phosphorus, which are critical for protecting high-compression engines under continuous 3,600 RPM operation. Generators don’t experience the stop-start cycles of a car; they run hot, under load, for hours. Standard automotive oil can break down faster, leading to sludge, valve train wear, and premature bearing failure. Stick to your manufacturer’s viscosity grade, but 10W-40 diesel-grade is your most reliable safeguard in Australia’s demanding thermal environment.

### Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How often should I change the generator oil?**  
A: Every 50–100 hours of operation or annually, whichever comes first. If you run it during prolonged blackouts, in dusty conditions, or at high altitudes, reduce that interval by half. Fresh oil means better heat transfer, fewer contaminants, and longer engine life.

**Q: Can I use synthetic oil instead of conventional?**  
A: Yes, and it’s highly recommended for Australian summers. Synthetic 10W-40 or 15W-50 maintains viscosity in extreme heat, flows faster at cold starts, and resists thermal breakdown. Just ensure it meets API CJ-4 or CK-4 standards and is compatible with your fuel type.

**Q: What’s the safest way to store fuel for long-term standby use?**  
A: Use UN-approved, sealable containers with fuel stabiliser added every 3–4 months. Store in a cool, shaded, well-ventilated area away from ignition sources. Never exceed 250L of petrol or 500L of diesel on residential property without checking local council and fire authority regulations.

**Q: My generator won’t start after sitting for months. What’s the first thing to check?**  
A: Fuel degradation and a clogged air filter. Stale fuel causes varnish in the carburetor or injectors. Drain old fuel, replace the spark plug or clean the glow plug, verify the oil level, and ensure the air filter is clean before attempting another start.

**Q: Is it worth paying a professional for annual servicing?**  
A: Absolutely. DIY maintenance covers basics; a qualified technician will test compression, inspect alternator windings, verify voltage regulation, check exhaust integrity, and calibrate the automatic transfer switch. Catching wear early prevents catastrophic failure when you need it most.

### Conclusion

Generator ownership isn’t about buying a machine and hoping it fires when the grid fails. It’s about disciplined, routine care. The oil change you just completed, the fuel management you’ve implemented, and the safety protocols you’ve followed are what separate a reliable power backup from a costly paperweight. In a country where heatwaves, storms, and grid instability are becoming the new normal, your generator is only as good as your last maintenance session. Treat it with respect, follow the manufacturer’s intervals, and never cut corners on ventilation, fire safety, or proper waste disposal. When the lights go out and the rest of the country is waiting for a return, you’ll be the one flipping a switch and keeping your family, your fridge, and your peace of mind running smoothly. That’s not just preparedness—it’s engineered confidence.
