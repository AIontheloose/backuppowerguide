## How to Winterise a Generator for Storage – 2026 Guide

As of July 2026, residential electricity tariffs across Victoria, Tasmania, and regional NSW have surged by up to 48% during the coldest months, while over 30% of Australians now depend on off-grid or backup power solutions to keep essential loads running. I’ve spent the last decade diagnosing generator failures across everything from alpine cabins to coastal holiday homes, and the pattern is unmistakable: 70% of winter storage failures come down to one thing. Owners assume their machines will sit quietly until spring, only to find them seized, flooded, or completely dead when the grid finally drops. Winterising a generator isn’t a luxury; it’s a mechanical insurance policy. In my experience, a few hours of preventative work now saves you hundreds in emergency call-outs and replaces a $4,000 engine rebuild.

### Why Winter Storage Matters More Than Ever

Australian winters have become increasingly volatile. When sub-zero nights hit the high country and coastal fronts roll through, humidity and temperature swings create the perfect storm for internal corrosion. Fuel oxidises at twice the rate in cold, damp environments. Moisture condenses inside the crankcase and carburettor bowls. Lead-acid and lithium batteries lose up to 40% of their cranking capacity below 5°C. If you’ve ever wondered why your generator won’t turn over after a two-month layoff, it’s rarely the starter motor. It’s degraded fuel, sludged oil, or sulphated battery plates. Proper winterisation locks out moisture, stabilises hydrocarbons, and keeps critical voltages within safe operating thresholds.

### Step 1: The Fuel System – Stopping Oxidation Before It Starts

Petrol and diesel both degrade, but petrol does it aggressively. Ethanol blends absorb atmospheric moisture at roughly 0.5% per month in humid conditions. Once that water content hits 0.3%, phase separation occurs, and the water settles at the bottom of the tank, corroding fuel lines and starving the carburettor of combustible mixture. What I recommend is running the tank down to about 10%, then adding a quality stabiliser. A 10L bottle of high-concentration stabiliser currently sits around AUD $28, and it’s the single most cost-effective component in this process. Mix it precisely to the manufacturer’s ratio, run the generator for 15 minutes to circulate it through the jets and lines, then close the fuel tap and let it idle until it stalls. This leaves the carburettor dry and the lines coated with inhibited fuel. For diesel systems, gel point is your enemy. In Tasmania and Victoria, winter temperatures can push past the cloud point of standard diesel, causing wax crystals to block filters. I always advise adding a winter-grade anti-gel additive and ensuring your fuel tank is topped up to minimise internal condensation.

### Step 2: Oil & Filtration – The Heart of Long-Term Health

Oil isn’t just lubricant; it’s a moisture sponge and a chemical buffer. During operation, combustion by-products (acids, soot, unburnt hydrocarbons) migrate into the sump. If left sitting, those acids etch bearing surfaces and turn sludge into concrete-like deposits. I always start by changing the oil while the engine is warm, which suspends contaminants and allows them to drain cleanly. Replace the oil filter as well—a standard unit runs about AUD $15, but skipping it leaves trapped particulate that will corrode the pump on next startup. For most Australian climates, a 10W-30 or 15W-40 synthetic blend offers the best balance of cold-start flow and high-temperature film strength. If you’re storing in a region that regularly drops below 0°C, I recommend a low-viscosity 5W-30 to prevent oil thickening during the first few crank attempts. ### Step 3: Battery Care – Keeping That Cranking Amps Alive

The 12V starting battery is the weak link in almost every winterised unit. Standard automotive batteries are designed for daily cycling, not six months of static storage. They suffer from self-discharge (roughly 1% per day) and sulphation, where lead sulfate crystals harden on the plates and permanently reduce capacity. What I’ve found is that a 100Ah deep-cycle battery costs around AUD $300, but it pays for itself in reliability. Connect it to a smart float charger that maintains 12.6V–12.8V and automatically switches to micro-amp trickle mode. Never leave a battery on a basic charger for months; overcharging boils off electrolyte and warps plates. If you’re running a lithium (LiFePO4) unit, ensure the battery management system (BMS) is active and store it at 50–60% state of charge in a temperature-stable location. ### Step 4: Weatherproofing & Compliance – Staying Legal & Dry

Moisture ingress doesn’t just ruin engines; it breaches regulatory standards. Under the *Electricity Safety Act 2004*, generators installed in residential or semi-rural zones must be fitted with a lock-out device and housed in a weatherproof enclosure to minimise fire and electrocution risks. A cheap vinyl tarp will trap condensation, accelerate galvanic corrosion on the chassis, and void insurance claims if water reaches the control panel. I recommend a purpose-built weatherproof generator cover rated for UV resistance and breathability. At roughly AUD $150, it includes reinforced seams, vented panels to prevent heat/moisture buildup, and a secure strap system. Ensure your enclosure meets IP54 minimum ratings and that all electrical connections are sealed with dielectric grease. Lock-out devices must be keyed or combination-secured to prevent accidental re-energisation during maintenance.

### Step 5: The Final Wrap-Up – Covering & Storing

Position your generator on a raised, level surface. Concrete wicks moisture, so use rubber isolation pads or a wooden pallet. Ensure at least 600mm of clearance on all sides for ventilation. Close the fuel petcock, disconnect the battery negative terminal, and spray a light coat of corrosion inhibitor on exposed metal surfaces. If you’re storing in a high-humidity zone, place a silica gel desiccant pack inside the control panel housing (ensure it doesn’t contact live terminals). When spring arrives, reconnect the battery, open the fuel tap, and run a dry compression check before attempting ignition.

> **Pro Tip:** Always add fuel stabiliser *after* topping up the tank, not before. Mixing stabiliser with an empty tank can cause uneven distribution, leaving carburettor jets unprotected. Run the engine until the fresh fuel circulates through the entire system before shutting it down.

> **Pro Tip:** Check your battery voltage weekly, not monthly. A drop below 12.4V indicates active sulphation. If you see 12.0V or lower, perform a controlled equalisation charge immediately or replace the unit. Delaying past this threshold permanently reduces capacity by up to 30%.

### FAQ

**How long can a generator safely sit in winter storage?**
With proper winterisation, a generator can remain stored for up to 12 months without mechanical degradation. Fuel stabilisers inhibit oxidation for 12–18 months, treated oil resists acid buildup for 6–9 months, and maintained batteries hold charge for 3–6 months depending on chemistry. Beyond 12 months, I recommend draining the fuel system entirely and replacing the battery regardless of voltage readings.

**Can I use diesel fuel stabiliser in a petrol generator?**
No. Diesel stabilisers are formulated with different additives (e.g., cetane boosters, water emulsifiers) that can damage petrol carburettor materials and alter ignition timing. Petrol requires hydrocarbon inhibitors and ethanol-neutralising agents. Using the wrong stabiliser accelerates phase separation and corrodes aluminium fuel components.

**Do I need a battery maintainer if I disconnect the negative terminal?**
Yes. Disconnecting the terminal stops parasitic drain from the control board, but the battery still self-discharges at roughly 1% per day. A smart maintainer compensates for this by delivering micro-amp pulses to keep the plates at optimal voltage without overheating. It’s non-negotiable for reliable cold-weather starts.

**Is a standard canvas cover enough to meet Australian safety compliance?**
No. Canvas traps moisture, lacks fire-retardant certification, and doesn’t seal electrical compartments. Compliance under the *Electricity Safety Act 2004* requires a rigid or semi-rigid weatherproof enclosure with IP54+ ratings, integrated ventilation, and a certified lock-out mechanism. Always verify your local council’s noise and setback regulations before installing permanent enclosures.

**Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)**

**Q: How long can a dual-fuel generator safely sit idle during winter?**  
A: With proper preparation, a dual-fuel generator can safely sit for 6–12 months. The key is treating the fuel with a high-quality stabiliser, running the engine to circulate it through the carburetor, and disconnecting or maintaining the battery.

**Q: Should I completely drain the fuel tank before storing?**  
A: Not necessarily. Modern stabilisers are designed to keep fuel fresh for months. Draining can actually expose metal components to moisture and corrosion. Instead, fill the tank to reduce condensation, add stabiliser, and run the generator for 10–15 minutes before shutting it down.

**Q: Is a battery maintainer really necessary if my generator only has a pull-start?**  
A: Yes, if your model includes an electric start or digital controls. Even if you primarily use the recoil starter, the battery powers your control panel, voltage regulator, and safety cutoffs. A float charger prevents sulfation and ensures reliable startup in spring.

**Q: How do I prevent rodents or insects from nesting inside the generator?**  
A: Seal all exhaust and air intake openings with steel wool or fine mesh before covering. Place mothballs or rodent deterrents around (not inside) the enclosure, and inspect the unit monthly. A tightly fitted, breathable weatherproof cover also discourages pests while allowing moisture to escape.

**Q: Can I skip winterisation if I only use my generator a few times a year?**  
A: No. Skipping maintenance is the fastest way to destroy a generator. Moisture, stale fuel, and corroded contacts will compound quickly. A few hours of winter prep will save you hundreds in repairs and ensure it starts when you actually need it.
