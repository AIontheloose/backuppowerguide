### Why Your Inspection Matters More Than Ever

Termites don't just eat wood; they eat equity. In 2026, with building material costs remaining stubbornly high, repairing termite damage is more expensive than ever. What starts as a few mud tubes in the subfloor can quickly turn into structural compromise to your skirting boards, floor joists, and cabinetry. The beauty of inspection is that termites are slow but steady. By the time you see major damage, they've likely been there for months. Regular checks allow you to catch them when they're just starting to establish, which is when your options are widest and costs are lowest. Whether you're in a humid coastal suburb or a dry inland town, every Australian home is at risk. The key is being proactive, not panicked.

### Step-by-Step: The Sarah Chen Inspection Routine

Grab your torch and let's walk through the inspection. I break this down into three zones: Exterior, Interior, and Hidden Spaces.

#### 1. The Exterior Perimeter Sweep
Start your inspection outside. Walk the entire perimeter of your home. Look for "mud tubes" on the foundation, brickwork, or retaining walls. These are pencil-thick tunnels made of soil and saliva that termites use to travel. They can be grey or brown and might look like dried mud streaks. Check the junction where timber meets brick. This is a common entry point. Also, inspect any wooden fencing or gate posts close to the house. If the wood feels soft or crumbly when you poke it with your screwdriver, dig deeper. Look for discarded wings near windowsills or door frames; these are shed wings from swarmers (reproductive termites) that have taken flight, usually after rain.

#### 2. Inside the Walls and Under the House
Move to the interior. Tap along your skirting boards, architraves, and window frames. Sound timber gives a solid thud; termite-damaged timber often sounds hollow or papery. Check for blistered paint or paint that is peeling away, which can indicate moisture buildup from within. If you have subfloor access, this is where the action often happens. Crawl in with your torch and inspect the ground line. Look for mud tubes running up the stumps or along the perimeter. Check for dark, damp patches on the ground or on the underside of the floorboards. In my inspections, I've found that 80% of termite issues start with a moisture problem in the subfloor, so pay extra attention to ventilation.

#### 3. Roof Cavity and Attic Check
Don't forget the roof space. Termites can travel up through the walls and enter from above. Check the rafters and battens for mud tubes or damaged timber. Look for "frass" (termite droppings), which looks like fine sawdust or pellets. Also, inspect where pipes and wires enter the house from the roof; gaps here are prime entry points.

> **Pro Tip:** If you find evidence of termites, do not spray them with insecticide. This can cause the colony to scatter and move deeper into your home, making the problem worse and harder to treat. Document the evidence with photos and call a licensed professional immediately.

### Frequently Asked Questions

**1. How often should I inspect my home for termites?**
You should conduct a thorough DIY inspection at least twice a year, ideally in the spring and autumn when termite activity tends to peak. If you live in a high-risk area or have had a history of infestations, quarterly checks are highly recommended. Professional inspections should ideally happen annually to ensure that hidden areas are covered by an expert eye.

**2. Can I treat a termite infestation myself?**
While you can buy over-the-counter sprays or baits, I strongly advise against attempting a full treatment yourself. Termites are highly organised, and DIY methods often fail to reach the queen or the main colony, leading to a scattered and more aggressive outbreak. Professional treatments require licensed chemicals and techniques that ensure the barrier is continuous and effective.

**3. What are the most common signs of termites in an Australian home?**
The most common signs include mud tubes on walls or foundations, hollow-sounding timber when tapped, discarded swarm wings near windowsills, and blistered or peeling paint that mimics water damage. You might also notice difficulty opening or closing doors and windows due to timber swelling, or a faint clicking sound coming from within the walls at night.

**4. Does home insurance cover termite damage?**
Unfortunately, most standard home insurance policies in Australia classify termite damage as a maintenance issue rather than sudden or accidental damage. This means you are typically responsible for the full cost of treatment and repairs. However, some policies offer optional pest damage cover or building warranty extensions that you can add for an annual fee, so it is always worth reviewing your policy documents carefully.

### Final Thoughts

Inspecting your home for termites doesn't have to be a daunting, expensive ordeal. By committing to a simple twice-yearly routine and keeping an eye on moisture

levels around your property, you’ll catch early warning signs before they escalate into costly structural repairs. Look for mud tubes, discarded wings, and hollow-sounding timber, and don’t hesitate to call in a licensed pest inspector if you spot anything suspicious. Prevention is always cheaper than cure, and a little vigilance goes a long way.
