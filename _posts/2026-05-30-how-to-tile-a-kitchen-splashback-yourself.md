## Step‑by‑Step Guide

### 1. Planning & Measurement

1. **Mark the area** – Use a chalk line and a spirit level to create a perfectly straight baseline on the wall. A level line is crucial; if your start point is off, your tiles will drift as you go up.
2. **Measure the height** – From the edge of the stove to the ceiling or the top of the cabinet.
3. **Calculate tile quantity** – Add 10% extra for cuts and waste.
   *Example:* A 1.2 m × 1.5 m splashback (1.8 m²) will need about 2 m² of tiles.

> **Pro Tip:** Keep a small spreadsheet or notebook handy to jot down measurements. It saves you from double‑buying or, worse, running out of tiles halfway through.

### 3. Prepare the Wall

Before you even think about grout, we need to talk about the wall. It's easy to get excited and start tiling over a dodgy surface, but trust me, your future self will thank you. Give that wall a good once-over. Tap it; does it sound hollow? That's a red flag. Check for hairline cracks, especially near the stove where heat expansion is constant. If you spot any, patch them with a quality filler and sand smooth.

Also, moisture is the silent enemy. If you're tiling near a sink or in a humid kitchen, ensure the substrate is completely dry. A damp wall can trap moisture behind the tiles, leading to mould or adhesive failure. Finally, use your 2-in-1 level to check for flatness. If the wall bows out more than a few millimetres, you might need a skim coat of plaster to get a truly even base. Once the wall is sound, apply your waterproof membrane over the entire area, cutting it to fit around edges. Let it cure for 24 hours as per the manufacturer's instructions.

### 4. Apply Adhesive

1. **Mix the adhesive** – Stir until a uniform, creamy consistency is achieved.
2. **Spread with a notched trowel** – Apply a 10 mm notch to ensure full coverage.
3. **Work in sections** – Do not spread adhesive over the entire wall at once. Work in manageable sections of about 1 m² to prevent the adhesive from skinning over before you lay the tiles.

### 5. Lay the Tiles

1. **Place the first tile** – Align with your baseline.
2. **Use spacers** – Insert spacers between tiles for consistent gaps.
3. **Check alignment** – Use your spirit level frequently to ensure the tiles remain straight.
4. **Press firmly** – Ensure the tile is fully embedded in the adhesive.

> **Pro Tip:** If you're using large format tiles, a back-buttering technique (applying adhesive to the back of the tile) can help prevent hollow spots.

### 6. Cutting & Fitting

Now, let's talk cuts. This is where many DIYers feel a twinge of anxiety, but you've got this. For straight cuts, a manual tile cutter is your best friend. It's affordable, quiet, and gives a crisp score-and-snap line perfect for 800×800mm porcelain tiles. You just mark your line, score it firmly, and apply gentle pressure to snap. However, if you have complex shapes, like cutting around a gas stove knob or a curved cabinet edge, a wet saw is the way to go. A wet saw uses a diamond blade cooled by water to slice through tough porcelain like butter.

If you don't want to buy one, most hardware stores in 2026 offer wet saw hire for a fraction of the cost. When cutting, always wear your safety mask and goggles; tile dust is no joke. For angled cuts around appliances, measure twice and cut once. Mark your cut line clearly with a pencil, and if you're cutting a corner, a miter cut or a slanted edge can hide the joint beautifully, giving that professional finish we all strive for. Take your time here, and don't rush the measurement phase.

### 7. Grouting

1. **Wait 24 hours** – Let the adhesive cure.
2. **Mix grout** – Follow the manufacturer's instructions.
3. **Apply with a rubber float** – Press grout into the joints at a 45° angle.
4. **Remove excess** – Use a damp sponge to wipe off excess grout from the tile surface.
5. **Clean up** – Once the grout is slightly dry, buff the tiles with a soft cloth.

> **Pro Tip:** Keep a separate bucket of clean water for rinsing the sponge. This prevents grout from drying on the tile and becoming difficult to remove.

### 8. Sealing & Finishing

Once your grout has cured for about 24 hours, don't skip the sealing stage. This is your splashback's shield. Apply a penetrating grout sealer using a small brush or a sponge applicator. Work it into the joints until the grout stops absorbing the liquid. Wipe off any excess from the tile surface immediately to avoid haze. This step is crucial in a kitchen where grease, tomato sauce, and splashes are daily visitors. A sealed splashback wipes clean in seconds, keeping your hard work looking fresh for years. Finally, remove any remaining spacers and caulk the edges with a silicone sealant that matches your grout colour for a polished look.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Skipping wall prep** – A poor substrate leads to tile failure. Always check for cracks and moisture.
- **Ignoring the waterproof membrane** – Essential for longevity, especially near water sources.
- **Rushing the adhesive cure** – Give it the full 24 hours before grouting.
- **Using the wrong adhesive** – Ensure it's suitable for the tile type (e.g., porcelain vs. ceramic).
- **Forgetting to seal the grout** – Without sealing, grout can stain and crack over time.

## FAQ

**Q1: How long does it take

to complete a 2m² project?

**A:** For a 2m² area, the physical installation typically takes **4 to 6 hours** for a beginner. However, patience is key: you **must wait 24 hours** for the adhesive to cure before grouting. After grouting, allow another 24 hours before sealing. Plan for a **two-day timeline** to ensure a durable finish. Rushing this process is the most common cause of bond failure.

**Q2: Can I tile over existing tiles?**

**A:** Generally, no. Tiling over old surfaces adds height and weight, which can compromise the bond and stress the substrate. Always remove existing tiles and ensure the base is solid, flat, and clean. If the floor is uneven, use a self-leveling compound before starting.

**Q3: How do I prevent "lippage" (uneven tile edges)?**

**A:** Use a **tile leveling system** (clips and wedges). These are inexpensive but make a massive difference, ensuring every tile sits perfectly flush with its neighbours. Also, check your adhesive coverage by lifting a test tile after 10 minutes; you should see 85–90% coverage on the back of the tile.
