## How to Claim Car Expenses on Your Australian Tax Return (2026)

Did you know the average Australian vehicle owner leaves roughly $1,200 on the table each tax year by claiming car expenses incorrectly? I see it daily in my workshop near Dubbo, where corrugated stock routes and dusty outback tracks chew through suspension components faster than any CBD commute. In 2026, the ATO has bumped the cents-per-km rate to 72 cents, but that flat rate is a tax trap for anyone clocking serious kilometres or hammering unsealed roads. I’m Dan Cooper, your Automotive Contributor, and I’m here to cut through the ATO jargon, keep your paperwork audit-proof, and make sure you’re claiming every lawful deduction without overpaying your mechanic or the tax office.

### Choosing the Right Claim Method: Logbook vs Cents-Per-Km

The biggest blunder I see isn’t about which car you drive—it’s picking a claim method because it sounds easier, not because it actually saves you money. Let’s break down the two main pathways the ATO recognises in 2026.

**The Logbook Method: Where the Real Money Lives**
If you’re driving for work, the logbook method is your gold standard. You claim a percentage of your total running costs based on a continuous 12-week logbook. The beauty of it? It captures fuel, rego, insurance, servicing, tyres, and vehicle depreciation. But here’s the part most folks miss: if 25% or more of your kilometres are on unsealed roads, the ATO acknowledges the extra wear and tear. While the ATO doesn’t prescribe a fixed percentage, industry practice and past rulings often see an additional 10–15% added to servicing and tyre claims to account for dust ingestion and suspension fatigue. I’ve successfully claimed an extra 15% on shock absorbers and brake components for vehicles regularly tackling the Gibb River Road.

You don’t need a fancy app, but you do need discipline. Record start/end positions, trip purpose, and kilometres for every journey. **Cents-Per-Km: The Set-and-Forget Option**
For low-kilometre drivers, the 72c/km method is a lifesaver. It bundles fuel, rego, insurance, depreciation, and maintenance into one flat rate. No running-cost receipts required. But there’s a hard cap: you can only claim up to 5% of the vehicle’s purchase price. Take a 2026 Toyota Corolla at $28,200. Five percent is $1,410. At 72c/km, that caps you at roughly 1,958km. Claim 2,000km? You still only get $1,410. Cross that threshold, and the logbook method almost always pays out better. I recommend this method only if your business use stays firmly under that 5% limit.

### FAQ: Car Expense Claims in 2026

**Can I claim car expenses if I work from home?**
Yes, but only for the portion of your commute that’s genuinely work-related, such as travelling between client sites or carrying specialised equipment. The ATO draws a strict line between ordinary commuting (non-deductible) and business travel (deductible). You must keep a logbook or use the cents-per-km method for those business trips, and you cannot claim both the home office space claim and car travel for the same kilometres.

**How do I accurately separate private and business kilometres for my claim?**
The ATO requires a minimum 12-week continuous logbook that reflects your typical driving patterns. Digital tracking apps are now fully accepted, provided they automatically record date, time, odometer readings, and trip purpose. Once you’ve established your business-use percentage, apply it consistently to all running costs if using the actual cost method, or simply multiply total business kilometres by the ATO’s annual cents-per-kilometre rate.

**Can I claim fuel, registration, and insurance separately?**
Only if you use the actual cost method. If you choose the cents-per-kilometre method (up to 5,000 business kilometres per year), the rate is all-inclusive—you cannot claim additional fuel, servicing, or insurance costs for those kilometres. Switching methods between financial years requires careful recalibration of your logbook and depreciation schedules.

**What happens if the ATO audits my car expense claims?**
The ATO cross-references your claims with registration records, fuel card data, and GPS telemetry where available. Maintain dated receipts, service invoices, and a contemporaneous logbook for at least five years. If discrepancies arise, the burden of proof is entirely on you. Consistent, contemporaneous records are your strongest defence.
