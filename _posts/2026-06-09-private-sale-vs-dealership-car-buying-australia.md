With interest rates stabilising but inflation still biting at family budgets, I've crunched the latest January 2026 market data and spoken to workshops across every state. Whether you're chasing a reliable daily or an EV to ride the new tax breaks, your choice between private and dealer can mean thousands in your pocket—or hundreds in unexpected repair bills. ### The Financial Breakdown: What You Actually Pay

The sticker price is only half the battle. When you run a **dealership price comparison** across the used market, you're looking at upfront savings versus risk mitigation. Take a 2018 Toyota Camry: reputable dealers list these around $28,000. That figure usually bundles a 12-month warranty and a service package worth roughly $1,200. On the private market, you're staring at approximately $20,500. That's a headline saving of $7,500. 

But hold your horses—I don't do maths without accounting for government levies. You still pay **stamp duty on private cars**, which in NSW sits at roughly $1.60 per $100 of purchase price. On a $20,500 private buy, that's about $328 in duty, plus registration hovering around $350 annually for a mid-size sedan. Dealers often advertise "drive-away" deals to mask this, but the tax burden inevitably follows the vehicle value. The bigger the machine, the more compelling the private route becomes. A 2021 Ford Ranger private seller wants $24,800, while dealers want $31,600. That's a $6,800 swing. Given diesel averages $1.80/L in 2026, that gap easily covers two years of coastal towing runs or long-haul freight.

### Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why do dealer prices consistently outpace private sales by 30–40%?**  
A: Dealers aren’t just reselling metal; they’re packaging compliance, reconditioning, and risk mitigation into a single figure. That margin covers mechanical inspections, RWC/roadworthy checks, advertising, finance commission structures, and warranty buffers. Private sales strip all that out, which is why the sticker looks tempting until you factor in immediate servicing or hidden fault repairs.

**Q: Is stamp duty really that important to calculate upfront?**  
A: Absolutely. While $328 on a Camry sounds manageable, it scales quickly with higher-value imports or luxury variants. Each state uses different thresholds and exemptions (e.g., commercial registrations in QLD vs. NSW), and some regional councils add surcharges. Always verify with your state’s revenue office before signing anything—duty is rarely refundable.

**Q: Should I bite the bullet for dealer add-ons like warranties?**  
A: Only if you plan to keep the car past its factory coverage. For a 2018 Camry or 2020 i30, a capped-price service plan or extended warranty often costs less than two major repairs out-of-pocket. For the Ranger, given higher torque loads and commercial wear patterns, it’s practically non-negotiable if you’re towing, carrying payloads, or running it past 100,000km.

**Q: How do I verify those private sale prices are realistic?**  
A: Cross-reference RedBook Australia, CarsGuide market guides, and Facebook Marketplace filters for your postcode over a 30-day window. Private listings often inflate “guide prices” to anchor negotiations—actual transaction prices usually settle 5–10% lower if you’ve verified service history, title status, and independent pre-purchase inspections.

### Conclusion

The used-car market in early 2026 isn’t forgiving, but it’s far from broken—if you know where to look and how to calculate the true cost of ownership. That dealer premium isn’t just markup; it’s insurance against hidden defects, compliance headaches, and financing friction. Private sales demand patience, verification skills, and a willingness to walk away when the numbers don’t align. Whether you’re chasing daily reliability in a Camry, budget-friendly agility in an i30, or heavy-duty capability in a Ranger, your best move is to treat every transaction like a small business deal: audit the add-ons, factor in state-specific duties, and never let glossy photos override documented service history. Drive smart, keep your receipts, and remember—in this market, informed hesitation beats impulsive commitment every time.
