### 1. Choosing Your Chocolate Base

Before we get our spatulas out, let's talk chocolate. Confusion here can lead to cracked shells or dull finishes. In 2026, the options are fantastic, but you need to decide what suits your skill level and budget.

#### Whole Bars vs. Pre-Made Shell Packs
*   **Whole Bars:** Great for beginners who want full control over flavour. You'll melt these down to create your own shells from scratch. Look for high-cocoa butter content for the best snap.
*   **Pre-Made Shells:** These are convenient moulded chocolate cups that just need filling. Perfect for busy parents or those new to tempering.

### 2. Filling & Sealing Techniques

Now that you have your chocolate sorted, let's talk filling. Whether you're making shells to fill or using pre-made cups, the filling technique ensures your eggs hold together during the hunt.

#### How to Prepare Your Filling
*   **Mini Bars:** If using mini bars like Lotte (12-pack for $3.75), break them into pieces that fit inside your egg cavity without forcing them. For a smoother fill, you can crumble them slightly with clean hands or use a small food processor pulse.
*   **Crumble Method:** Place 30g of filling in the bottom of the shell. Tap the shell gently on the bench to settle the crumbs.
*   **Piping Bag Trick:** For wetter fillings like caramel or nut butter, use a small piping bag to squeeze the filling exactly where you want it. This prevents messy edges and keeps the egg clean.

#### Filling Volume Guide
> **Pro Tip:** Stick to roughly **30g of filling per egg**. This keeps the eggs light enough for little hands to carry without breaking but still bursting with flavour. Anything above 50g can cause the shell to crack due to pressure or make the egg top-heavy and prone to tipping during the hunt.

### 3. Safety First: Hygiene & Allergy Awareness

With **family baking activities** often involving little ones, safety is paramount. Chocolate is delicious, but we want everyone to stay happy and healthy.

*   **Allergy Checks:** Before you start, survey your family. Are there nut allergies? Consider swapping peanut butter fillings for sunflower seed butter or dairy-free alternatives. Always label homemade eggs clearly with ingredients when gifting.
*   **Hygiene Heroes:** Chocolate can harbour bacteria if mishandled. Ensure all bowls, spoons, and moulds are washed in hot soapy water. If children are helping, teach them the "no taste-testing from the raw chocolate bowl" rule to avoid cross-contamination.
*   **Temperature Safety:** While we temper chocolate, ensure kids stay clear of simmering water stations. Use long-handled spoons for stirring and keep handles turned inward to prevent bumps.

### 4. The Art of Tempering

Tempering is the secret sauce behind that glossy shine and satisfying snap. Without it, your eggs might look dull or feel greasy. In 2026, we have brilliant tools to make this foolproof.

#### Why These Temperatures Matter?
Chocolate contains cocoa butter crystals that must align perfectly. If you heat chocolate above its stable range, the crystals break down, leading to "bloom"—those unappealing white streaks. By cooling and reheating to specific temperatures, we encourage the formation of Type V crystals, which give us that professional finish.

#### Safety Warning
> **Pro Tip:** Monitor your thermometer closely! If you notice a sudden rise of more than 5°C while melting, remove the bowl from heat immediately and stir in chopped cool chocolate to bring the temperature down. Chocolate can scorch instantly; patience is your best friend here.

#### Tempering Steps Simplified
1.  **Melt:** Chop your chocolate finely. Place in a heat-proof bowl over simmering water (double boiler method) until it reaches 45°C for milk or 40°C for dark. Stir gently.
2.  **Cool:** Remove from heat. Add chopped room-temperature chocolate to the mixture and stir until it drops to 31–32°C (milk) or

27–28°C for dark chocolate. The added solid pieces act as “seeds,” introducing stable beta crystals without requiring you to cool the entire batch in a refrigerator or over-ice.

3. **Reheat & Maintain:** Gently return the bowl to indirect heat for just a few seconds at a time, stirring constantly until it climbs back to 31°C (milk) or 32–33°C (dark). Keep it within this narrow working window throughout your molding, dipping, or drizzling.

4. **Test Before Use:** Drag a butter knife through a small pool of chocolate on parchment paper. Properly tempered chocolate will set within 3–5 minutes at room temperature, drying to a smooth, glossy surface with a clean snap and no grey streaks.

#### Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why does my chocolate look dull or develop white streaks?**  
A: This is fat or sugar bloom, usually caused by improper crystal formation or exposure to moisture/heat fluctuations. Re-melt the batch and follow the seeding method carefully, ensuring you never exceed your target temperatures.

**Q: Can I temper chocolate in a microwave instead of a double boiler?**  
A: Yes, but it demands extreme caution. Use 10–15 second bursts at 50% power, stirring thoroughly between each interval. Microwaves heat unevenly, making temperature control significantly harder than with indirect steam heat.

**Q: How do I know if my chocolate has gone past tempering?**  
A: If it refuses to set within 5 minutes, feels sticky, or shows a matte/grey finish, the crystal structure has collapsed. You’ll need to re-melt and start over, though you can still use it for ganache, sauces, or drinking chocolate.

**Q: Does humidity affect tempering?**  
A: Absolutely. Temperatures above 24°C with high humidity will slow setting and encourage bloom. Work in a cool, dry room (ideally 18–20°C) and avoid running taps or opening windows near your workspace.
