#### 1. Slow-Cooked Beef and Pumpkin Stew
This is my absolute go-to for busy weeknights when the kids need a warm hug in a bowl. It's rich, satisfying, and costs less than $4 per serve.

**Ingredients:**
*   1 kg chuck steak, cut into 3 cm cubes
*   2 tbsp olive oil
*   1 large onion, diced
*   3 carrots, sliced
*   2 celery stalks, chopped
*   4 cloves garlic, crushed
*   4 beef stock cubes (dissolved in 500 ml hot water)
*   1 can (400 g) pumpkin puree
*   500 ml beef broth
*   Salt and freshly cracked black pepper to taste
*   **To Serve:** Mashed organic sweet potatoes

**Method:**
1.  Heat the olive oil in a large heavy-based pot or Dutch oven over medium-high heat. Season the chuck steak with salt and pepper, then brown in batches until golden. Remove and set aside.
2.  In the same pot, sauté the onion, carrots, and celery for five minutes until softened. Add the crushed garlic and cook for one more minute until fragrant.
3.  Return the beef to the pot. Stir in the pumpkin puree, dissolved stock cubes mixture, and beef broth. Bring to a simmer, then reduce heat to low.
4.  Cover and slow-cook on low for six hours, or if using an [Instant Pot Duo Evo Plus](https://www.amazon.com.au/s?k=Instant+Pot+Duo+Evo+Plus&tag=owlno-22), pressure cook for exactly 90 minutes to break down the collagen without drying out the meat.
5.  Taste and adjust seasoning. Serve ladled generously over creamy mashed organic sweet potatoes. The natural sweetness of the potatoes ($2.20 per pack) pairs beautifully with the savoury beef, and the little ones will devour every bite!

#### 2. Creamy Coconut Rosemary Lamb Roast
For a weekend treat or a special family dinner, this lamb roast brings that festive gathering feel to any Tuesday night. The coconut milk adds a luxurious creaminess that cuts through the richness of the meat.

**Ingredients:**
*   1.5 kg leg of lamb, butterflied and rolled
*   1 bunch fresh rosemary (≈30 g), needles stripped and chopped
*   1 head of garlic (approx. 10 g cloves), minced
*   2 tsp native lemon myrtle powder
*   400 ml can coconut milk
*   2 tbsp olive oil
*   Salt and pepper to taste
*   **Side:** Refreshing native mint slaw with capsicum

**Method:**
1.  Preheat your oven to 180 °C. In a small bowl, mix the chopped rosemary, minced garlic, lemon myrtle powder, coconut milk, olive oil, salt, and pepper to create a thick marinade.
2.  Rub the mixture all over the lamb, ensuring it gets into every nook and cranny. Let it rest for 30 minutes at room temperature to penetrate the flavours.
3.  Place the lamb in a roasting tin and cook for 1 hour 45 minutes, or until the internal temperature reaches 65 °C for medium-rare. Baste with pan juices every 20 minutes.
4.  Remove from the oven and rest under foil for at least 15 minutes to lock in the juices. Slice thinly against the grain and serve with the native mint slaw. The rosemary cuts through the richness, while the native mint adds a refreshing contrast that highlights our wonderful produce.

### Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I substitute cornstarch for plain flour when making a slurry?**  
A: You can, but adjust carefully. Cornstarch is roughly twice as potent as wheat flour, so use only 60 g and whisk it into cold liquid first to avoid clumping. Also, note that cornstarch-thickened sauces lose viscosity faster upon prolonged reheating.

**Q: My sauces still split even when I add a slurry. What am I doing wrong?**  
A: Overheating is the usual culprit. Once your sauce reaches the desired thickness, immediately reduce the heat to low and never let it boil vigorously. Gentle simmering keeps the starch granules intact and the emulsion stable.

**Q: Is 15 minutes really enough resting time for a large roast?**  
A: Yes. The general rule is 10 minutes per kilogram plus an additional 5 minutes. This window allows muscle fibres to relax and juices to migrate back toward the centre, preventing them from spilling out onto your cutting board.

**Q: Can I use frozen root vegetables if that’s all I have?**  
A: Frozen roots release too much water during roasting, which steams rather than caramelises them. If you must use them, thaw completely, pat thoroughly dry, and toss with a light coating of oil before spreading them on a preheated tray.
