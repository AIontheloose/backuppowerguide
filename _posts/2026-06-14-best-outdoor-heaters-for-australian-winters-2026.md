### Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do certain pavers interfere with patio heater safety or efficiency?**  
A: Absolutely. Non-combustible, heat-resistant pavers like concrete, travertine, porcelain, or sandstone are ideal near both electric and propane units. Avoid timber-look composites, resin-bound surfaces, or lightweight clay tiles within 1m of high-output heaters, as prolonged thermal exposure can cause warping, adhesive failure, or surface blistering.

**Q: What’s the minimum paver thickness for heavy outdoor heaters?**  
A: I recommend a minimum of 20mm for standard patio setups, but if you’re installing a freestanding unit over 3 kW or mounting it near structural edges, opt for 30–40mm thickened concrete or natural stone to prevent micro-cracking from thermal stress and foot traffic.

**Q: Are electric outdoor heaters safer than propane on paver surfaces?**  
A: Generally, yes. Electric units produce radiant or convective heat with no open flame, combustion byproducts, or oxygen depletion risk, making them far more forgiving on sensitive paver joints and sealants. Propane heaters require strict clearance protocols (minimum 1m from walls/foliage) and proper ventilation—never use them in enclosed pergolas or near flammable landscaping.

**Q: How do I clean heated pavers without damaging the surface?**  
A: Stick to pH-neutral cleaners and soft-bristle brushes. Avoid acidic descalers, bleach, or high-pressure washing above 1,500 PSI, as repeated thermal cycling already stresses the matrix. Reapply a penetrating silane/siloxane sealer annually to protect against UV fading and moisture ingress.

**Q: Is it legal to install patio heaters in rental properties?**  
A: Tenancy laws don’t regulate outdoor heating directly, but landlords must ensure any fixed or high-output installations comply with local council regulations and Australian Standards AS/NZS 5601 (gas) or AS 3000 (electric). Always get written approval before hardwiring units or modifying outdoor structures.
