### Environmental & Code Considerations Before You Shop

Before we dive into fixtures, let’s talk about what actually keeps them working reliably here. Australia’s climate is unforgiving on electronics, and skipping these basics will cost you more in replacements down the track.

* **Coastal vs Inland Exposure:** Salt spray accelerates corrosion on cheap steel brackets and degrades plastic housings. If you live within 1 km of the coast, insist on **IP66 outdoor fixtures** or higher, with stainless‑steel or marine‑grade aluminium mounts.
* **NCC compliance lighting:** The National Construction Code (Volume 2, Part 3.4) outlines minimum illumination levels for pathways and entryways. Most councils also enforce light‑pollution bylaws that restrict upward glare and require shields on high‑output fixtures. Always check your local planning guidelines before mounting anything above 2.5 metres.
* **Temperature swings:** Inland summer heat can push junction boxes past safe operating temperatures, while winter dampness invites condensation inside poorly sealed housings. Look for UV‑stabilised polymers and silicone gaskets rather than standard acrylic covers.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How long do solar path light batteries last?**  
Most lithium-ion packs retain 70% capacity for 2–3 years before replacement becomes necessary. Look for models with swappable cells to extend overall system life without discarding the entire fixture.

**Can I use solar lights in heavy shade or coastal areas?**  
Partial shading drastically reduces charging efficiency, so reserve these for sunny borders or elevated mounts. Coastal installations require IP65+ ratings and stainless-steel or marine-grade aluminium hardware to resist salt corrosion.

**Do I need a licensed electrician for any outdoor lighting upgrade?**  
Only if you’re hardwiring into your switchboard, modifying existing circuits, or installing low-voltage transformers near plumbing. Standalone solar and plug-in LED kits are fully DIY-friendly under AS/NZS 3000 guidelines.

**What’s the difference between warm white and cool white garden LEDs?**  
Warm white (2700K–3000K) reduces light pollution, minimises insect attraction, and complements natural foliage. Cool white (4000K–5000K) improves contrast and visibility, making it better for security zones or busy pathways.
