## How to Run a Generator Safely Indoors in Australia (2026)

If you’ve watched the latest bushfire warnings or grid stress alerts on the evening news and thought, “I’ll just wheel my generator into the garage,” you’re not alone. In 2026, over thirty percent of Australian households keep a portable power unit ready for blackouts. But running a combustion engine indoors is not a casual fix-it-yourself job. It requires strict adherence to ventilation protocols, electrical isolation standards, and fuel safety guidelines. Below I’ll walk you through exactly what it takes to operate a generator inside your property without risking carbon monoxide poisoning, fire, or non-compliance with Australian building codes.

---

### 1. The Real Risk: Why Indoor Use Demands Rigour

The core issue isn’t just wattage; it’s exhaust volume and room air exchange. Any portable generator producing combustion will emit carbon monoxide (CO), a colourless, odourless gas that displaces oxygen in the bloodstream. The old rule of thumb suggesting “1 kW is safe indoors” is misleading. That threshold originally referred to CO detector sensitivity limits, not engine safety. In reality, even a 1.5 kW inverter generator at idle produces roughly 60–80 litres of exhaust per minute. Without engineered ventilation, those gases pool at floor level, seep through door gaps, and infiltrate living spaces within minutes.

Australian Standard AS/NZS 2293 explicitly warns against unventilated indoor operation. Back-drafting occurs when internal air pressure drops (often from extractor fans or HVAC systems), pulling exhaust back toward occupants rather than pushing it out. Treat CO like a silent hazard: if you cannot guarantee direct external exhaust routing and continuous fresh-air exchange, the engine stays outside.

### 4. Venting & Exhaust Management

If you must run a combustion generator indoors, engineered venting is non-negotiable. Portable exhaust kits typically cost between $121 and $165 AUD and consist of heat-resistant aluminium ducting, a rain cap, and high-temperature silicone couplers.

**Placement rules:**
- Raise the unit on a vibration-dampening mat at least 30 cm off concrete to prevent heat trapping and oil pooling.
- Maintain a minimum 1.5 m clearance from doors, windows, vents, or any opening leading to habitable rooms.
- Route ducting in a straight line with no more than one 90° bend. Kinks reduce airflow by up to 40%, causing CO back-pressure.
- Seal all joints with fire-rated aluminium tape. Never use standard duct tape; it melts under exhaust temperatures exceeding 120°C.

Before ignition, verify the external vent cap faces downwind and sits at least 3 m from neighbouring properties or fresh-air intakes.

### 5. Carbon Monoxide Detection & Electrical Isolation

Detection requires more than a hallway alarm. Install a dual-sensor CO monitor (detecting both carbon monoxide and ambient temperature) on the ceiling directly opposite the generator, within 3 m. Australian standards demand ceiling mounting because CO mixes evenly with air but can stratify in poorly circulated rooms. Trigger thresholds should be set to alert at 50 ppm over one hour or 200 ppm instantly.

Electrical isolation is equally critical. Never back-feed your home’s switchboard without a certified transfer switch or isolation valve. Instead, run a dedicated 30 A RCD-protected circuit from the generator to a weatherproof outdoor outlet, then use heavy-gauge extension cords rated for continuous operation. Always pair the setup with a whole-system surge protector to guard against voltage spikes during grid restoration.

### 6. Fuel Storage, Maintenance & Environmental Impact

Fuel safety is often overlooked until it’s too late. Store diesel or petrol in AS 1940-compliant fireproof containers, kept in a shaded, well-ventilated shed at least 5 m from the generator and any ignition sources. Use spill containment trays with 110% of tank capacity to catch leaks during refuelling. Never refuel while the engine is hot or running; allow a full five-minute cool-down period.

Noise levels matter in suburban zones. Quality inverters operate at 48–52 dB(A) at 7 metres, but muffler degradation increases noise by up to

12–15 dB(A), easily breaching local council quiet-hours ordinances and neighbourly goodwill. Schedule annual exhaust inspections, replace cracked silencers promptly, and consider acoustic enclosures or rubberised mounting pads if you operate in a densely populated area. Environmental responsibility extends beyond decibels. Used engine oil, fuel filters, and degraded batteries must be handed to licensed hazardous waste recyclers—never poured down drains or buried on-site. Modern synthetic blends burn cleaner, reduce carbon fouling, and cut particulate emissions by up to 20%, extending service intervals while minimising your ecological footprint.

### Frequently Asked Questions

**How long can a portable generator run on a full tank?**  
Runtime depends on load and fuel capacity, but quality units typically deliver 8–12 hours at 50% load. Monitor consumption under your actual household demand rather than relying solely on manufacturer claims.

**Can I use petrol in a diesel generator or mix fuels to save money?**  
Never. Cross-fuelling causes immediate engine seizure, voids warranties, and creates severe safety hazards. Always verify the specification plate before purchasing fuel.

**How often should I service my backup generator?**  
Follow the manufacturer’s interval—usually every 100–200 operating hours or annually, whichever comes first. Core tasks include oil/filter changes, spark plug replacement (petrol), air filter cleaning, and a full load test to verify voltage stability under stress.

**Are inverter generators worth the premium?**  
For sensitive electronics like medical equipment, smart panels, or laptop workstations, yes. Inverters supply clean power with THD below 3%, preventing circuit damage from harmonic distortion and frequency fluctuations.
