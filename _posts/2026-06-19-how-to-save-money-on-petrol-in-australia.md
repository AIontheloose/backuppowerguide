## How to Save Money on Petrol in Australia (2026)

I just spent $97 on a 60‑litre tank at a suburban Sydney servo and actually didn’t wince when the pump clicked off. Why? Because I’ve stopped treating fuel bills like a fixed tax and started treating them like a variable cost I can actually influence. As of early 2026, the average retail price for regular unleaded across Australian capital cities and regional hubs sits at a stubborn **AUD 1.62 / L**, with diesel hovering around **AUD 1.70 / L**. Please note that prices fluctuate daily based on crude markets, excise adjustments, and local competition, so always check your nearest bowser before committing to a fill-up. That said, while we can’t magically shrink the price at the pump, we absolutely can shrink how much of it vanishes down the exhaust pipe. Over years of covering everything from corrugated outback tracks to gridlocked Melbourne arterials, I’ve learned that saving on fuel isn’t about gimmicky gadgets or making your car suffer—it’s about smart maintenance, realistic driving habits, and understanding what actually moves the needle on consumption.

### Master Your Driving Habits & Route Planning

The way you drive accounts for more than half of your fuel bills, yet it’s the cheapest area to fix. Australian roads demand a different approach depending on where you are. In city traffic, stop-start driving kills efficiency. I recommend keeping a steady pace and leaving a larger gap in front of you. That way, you’re coasting more and braking less. Every heavy brake application wastes kinetic energy that petrol just bought you.

When you’re heading interstate or tackling regional runs, distance planning becomes your best friend. The average Australian interstate trip exceeds 400 km, and let’s be honest—fuel stations can sit over 60 km apart once you leave the major corridors. I always map my route using offline-capable apps that show real-time pricing and station reliability. Filling up in regional towns or mining-adjacent hubs before heading into remote stretches isn’t just sensible; it’s a financial necessity. Prices often climb by 10–15% once you’re past the last major fuel corridor due to logistics, transport surcharges, and limited competition.

Climate plays a massive role here too. Southern states like Victoria and NSW experience intense summer heatwaves that force drivers to blast air-conditioning for hours on end. Industry trials and government transport studies generally place AC load between 5–10% in heavy traffic, scaling with cabin temperature and vehicle size. If you’re doing short urban runs under 60 km/h, crack a window at lower speeds. Once you hit highway speeds above 80 km/h, close the windows and run the AC—modern compressors are far more efficient than the aerodynamic drag of open glass.

Australian road conditions also silently drain your tank. Corrugations, potholes, and unsealed roads force your suspension to work overtime and cause constant micro-adjustments in steering and throttle. This increases tyre wear and rolling resistance. I always advise maintaining higher tyre pressures (within safe limits) on rough outback runs to reduce sidewall flex, and keeping a steady, moderate speed rather than accelerating through bumps. Smooth is fast; jerky is expensive.

> **Pro Tips for the Road**
> • Use cruise control on flat highways, but switch to manual or adaptive coasting when the road slopes downhill. Modern engines cut fuel injection entirely during deceleration in gear, so carrying speed into a gentle decline saves more petrol than braking and re-accelerating ever will.
> • Never fill past the first click of the nozzle. Forcing fuel past the overflow line damages vapor recovery systems and literally spills money onto hot bitumen.
> • Plan regional fuel stops 50 km before you hit remote stretches; logistics surcharges in outback towns routinely add 12–18% to base prices.

### Under the Bonnet – Maintenance That Pays Off

You can’t drive your way out of poor mechanical condition. I’ve seen too many drivers chase cheap fuel only to ignore basic maintenance, which quietly erodes efficiency. Let’s talk about what actually matters and what’s just marketing fluff.

First up: tyre pressure. Under-inflated tyres increase rolling resistance, which directly forces the engine to work harder. A standard digital tire pressure gauge costs around **AUD 15**, and I swear by checking pressures once a month—especially before long trips. Australian summer heat expands air inside your tyres, but cold fronts or altitude changes drop it fast. Keep them at the manufacturer’s recommended PSI (usually found on the driver’s door jamb), not the max pressure printed on the sidewall. For rough outback runs, bumping pressures up 5–10 kPa within safe limits reduces sidewall flex and improves highway economy without sacrificing ride comfort.

Engine oil is another silent efficiency killer. Using the wrong viscosity or pushing past service intervals creates internal drag. A quality 5 W‑30 synthetic blend (4 L) runs about **AUD 35**, and it can improve engine efficiency by roughly 1–2%. Combine that with a fresh air filter during your standard **AUD 70–90** service, and you’re looking at a noticeable drop in litres per 100 km. I recommend sticking to the manufacturer’s exact oil specification; “upgrading” to a heavier synthetic without cause can actually reduce cold-start efficiency and add unnecessary cost.

Weight matters more than people think. That toolbox full of half-used WD-40, old floor mats, and spare tyres in the boot? You’re carrying that around at 100 km/h every day. For every extra 50 kg, you lose roughly 1% in fuel economy. Clear out the boot, remove unnecessary roof racks when they’re not in use, and stop treating your hatchback like a storage unit.

### Fuel Types, Alternatives & Honest Cost Breakdowns

Yes, we can talk about LPG and EVs briefly, but let’s keep it grounded for 2026. Regular unleaded is priced at **AUD 1.62 / L**, with diesel sitting at **AUD 1.70 / L** due to higher refining demands and excise adjustments. LPG sits around **AUD 0.95 / L**, which sounds tempting, but the network is still patchy outside capital cities, and conversion kits cost upwards of $4,000—payback only works if you’re clocking 25,000+ km annually in a compatible engine.

Electric vehicles are pricing their way into reality. Off-peak home charging sits near **AUD 0.14–$0.16 / kWh** across most states, which translates to roughly a third of the cost per equivalent distance compared to petrol. For those not ready to plug in, hybrids remain the most pragmatic bridge. ### Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Are EV running costs really that low, or is it just off-peak charging?**  
A: The $0.15/kWh figure assumes you’re on a time-of-use tariff and charge overnight. Peak rates can push costs to $0.30–$0.40/kWh in some states, cutting your range-per-dollar roughly in half. Still, even at peak, EVs typically underperform petrol only in direct energy cost—not in maintenance or regenerative braking savings.

**Q: Do hybrids still make sense now that EVs are cheaper to run?**  
A: Absolutely. If you lack reliable home charging, do frequent long-haul trips, or live in a rental, a self-charging hybrid eliminates range anxiety while still delivering 20–30% lower running costs than conventional petrol models. They’re the smartest interim technology for most Australian households.

**Q: Is LPG becoming obsolete? Will I struggle to find stations?**  
A: While new factory-fitted LPG vehicles are rare, retrofitted systems remain viable. Major networks like Ampol and Shell still support LPG in metro areas, though regional coverage is patchy. If you drive under 15,000 km annually and stick to urban/suburban routes, LPG can still save you thousands over five years.

**Q: How do I properly compare running costs across fuel types?**  
A: Look beyond per-litre or per-kWh prices. Calculate your cost per 100km using your actual driving mix (city vs highway), factor in tyre wear, brake pad life (EVs last longer), and insurance premiums. Tools like the NRMA’s TCO calculator or CarsGuide’s running cost checker do the heavy lifting for you.

**Q: Can I safely charge an EV on a standard 10A household outlet?**  
A: Yes, but it’s slow (roughly 4–6 km of range per hour) and not ideal for daily use unless it’s your only option. Always use a certified Level 2 wallbox for regular charging—it cuts charging time by 80% and is safer for prolonged overnight use.

### Conclusion

At the end of the day, choosing your next fuel strategy isn’t about chasing hype—it’s about matching your daily routine, home setup, and budget to the technology that actually serves you. EVs are undeniably reshaping Australia’s motoring landscape, offering unprecedented running cost savings when charged smartly. But they’re not a one-size-fits-all solution yet. Hybrids remain the pragmatic workhorse for families juggling school runs, weekend trips, and limited charging access, while LPG still holds niche value for budget-conscious drivers in established corridors. Don’t let marketing dictate your decision. Track your real-world kilometres, audit your home power options, and run the numbers on your specific model before signing on the dotted line. Drive smart, stay adaptable, and let efficiency follow logic—not trends.
