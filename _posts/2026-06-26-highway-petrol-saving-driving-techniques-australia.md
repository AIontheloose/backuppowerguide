## How to Save Every Litre on Australian Highways in 2026

Let’s clear up a common misconception straight away: the headline figure floating around about petrol costs isn’t charging you $1.80 per kilometre, but rather **$1.80 per litre** at the bowser. In 2026, with fuel excise holding steady and global crude markets stabilising, that average sits comfortably between $1.75 and $1.85 across most eastern states, dipping slightly in WA and QLD where refinery margins are thinner. On a 4,500-kilometre Perth-to-Sydney run, you’re looking at roughly $750–$850 on the nose if you drive as the car was engineered. But here’s the good news: Australian highways are long, flat, and forgiving if you know how to read them. By tweaking your driving habits, respecting a strict maintenance schedule, and skipping the marketing fluff around aftermarket gadgets, you can shave meaningful litres off your tank without turning every trip into a budget exercise.

I’ve spent years tracking fuel consumption across the Pacific Highway, the Barkly, and the Nullarbor. The data doesn’t lie: small adjustments compound fast. More importantly, burning less fuel isn’t just about keeping more cash in your pocket; it directly cuts your CO₂ emissions by roughly 2.3 kilograms per litre spared. On a long-haul trip, that’s half a tonne of greenhouse gases you’ve simply chosen not to release. Let’s talk about how to make that happen without compromising safety or comfort.

### 1️⃣ Vehicle Classes & The Real Cost of Speed

Not every vehicle responds to speed changes the same way. Aerodynamic drag scales with the square of velocity, so taller, boxier platforms pay a heavier penalty than sleeker sedans. Here’s how different classes behave on open asphalt:

| Vehicle Class | Optimal Cruising Speed | Fuel Economy Drop at 120 km/h vs 95 km/h | Typical Mod/Gadget Cost (AUD) |
|---------------|------------------------|--------------------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| Compact Sedan/Wagon | 90–95 km/h | ~1.8 L/100km increase | Air filter replacement: $35 |
| Mid-size SUV | 90–100 km/h | ~2.4 L/100km increase | Quality tyre inflator (12V): $45 |
| Full-size Ute/Pickup | 85–95 km/h | ~2.7 L/100km increase | OBD2 eco-monitoring device: $65 |
| Diesel vs petrol comparison | Diesel torque allows lower RPM cruising; petrol needs higher revs for similar load | Varies by engine architecture | Cruise control retrofit kit (installed): $135–$160 |

The takeaway? Lighter, lower-slung cars can safely flirt with 95 km/h without punishing their fuel efficiency. Utes and large SUVs need to stay closer to 85–90 km/h to keep rolling resistance and aerodynamic drag in check. ### 2️⃣ Hitting the Sweet Spot: Cruising Speed & Aerodynamics

The most underrated lever you have is your right foot. For most modern petrol engines, peak fuel efficiency sits between 85 and 95 km/h. Push past 100 km/h, and drag becomes your primary adversary. Industry testing by the NRMA and RACV consistently shows that every 10 km/h over 90 km/h typically costs an extra 0.6 to 0.9 L/100km on open roads. That’s not just a number; it’s roughly $2.50 per hour you’re setting on fire when the bowser reads $1.80/L.

> **Evidence-based tip:** Treat your speedometer like a throttle map. On multi-lane highways, maintaining 90–95 km/h in the left lane often matches or beats the time penalty of filtering through slower traffic while burning noticeably less fuel. When the gradient climbs or crosswinds pick up, ease back to 85 km/h. The engine doesn’t need to work as hard, and your stopping distance improves dramatically on hot, sun-bleached bitumen.

### 3️⃣ Maintenance That Actually Moves the Needle

You can drive perfectly, but a neglected drivetrain will still punish you at the bowser. Let’s talk brass tacks.

**Tyre Inflation Pressure & Heat Management**
Australian highways bake in summer, and tyre inflation pressure naturally expands as sidewall temperature climbs. A drop of just 1 psi below the manufacturer’s placard rating increases rolling resistance enough to cost roughly 0.02 L/100km. Over a long trip, that adds up. Always check pressures when tyres are cold, and carry a digital gauge plus a quality inflator. Don’t chase “higher than spec” numbers for marginal gains; modern radial compounds are calibrated precisely. Stick to the door jamb sticker or owner’s manual load rating.

**Engine Oil & Friction Dynamics**
Switching to a low-viscosity, high-synthetic blend like 5W-30 (ILSAC GF-6A standard) isn’t just about longevity; it’s about hydrodynamic film stability. Thinner oil circulates faster during cold starts and maintains optimal clearance at operating temperature, reducing parasitic drag on the crankshaft and camshaft. Manufacturers like Toyota and Ford publish technical service bulletins confirming that proper viscosity grades can improve mechanical efficiency by up to 0.1 L/100km on highway duty cycles. Change it every 10,000–15,000 km or per your maintenance schedule, whichever comes first.

**Air Filtration & Airflow**
A clogged cabin or intake filter doesn’t just affect the engine; it forces the MAF sensor to compensate by enriching the mixture. Replace your paper or cotton gauze element every 15,000 km, or sooner if you’re kicking up dust on unsealed shoulders. A fresh filter costs around $35 and typically restores lost volumetric efficiency within a few hundred kilometres.

### 4️⃣ Tech & Gadgets: Separating Hype from Reality

The aftermarket is flooded with “fuel-saving” dongles, but let’s be brutally honest about what actually works in 2026.

**Cruise Control on Open Highways**
On flat, predictable stretches, adaptive or standard cruise control eliminates micro-accelerations that manually driven cars average out to roughly 0.5–1% better fuel

...efficiency than constant thumb modulation. But don’t expect miracles on hilly terrain or in stop-and-go traffic—adaptive systems actually *waste* fuel trying to maintain gap distance in dense traffic. Save it for the open road, not the morning commute.

**What About Those “Fuel-Saving” Dongles?**
Plug-in devices claiming to optimize ignition timing, descale injectors, or “recalibrate your ECU” are exactly what you’d expect: zero empirical backing and a lot of return requests. Modern ECUs already run 100+ parameters in real-time, adjusting for temperature, load, altitude, and fuel quality. A $29 USB stick can’t outsmart an OEM calibration team that’s spent decades tuning for emissions, longevity, and drivability. If it worked, manufacturers would’ve installed it standard.

**Tire Pressure Monitoring Systems (TPMS) Are Your Friend**
Don’t just watch the warning light—use it. A 0.3 bar drop across all four tires can cost you 2–3% in rolling resistance. Check pressure cold, monthly, and don’t forget the spare. Run them at the door-jamb spec, not the sidewall maximum.

### ❓ FAQ: Fuel Efficiency Myths & Realities

**Q: Does premium fuel actually improve mileage in regular engines?**  
A: Only if your manufacturer specifies it. Otherwise, you’re just paying extra for higher octane that your ECU doesn’t need. Modern knock sensors handle lower grades just fine.

**Q: Is idling for more than 30 seconds worse than restarting the engine?**  
A: Yes. Modern fuel-injected systems use less than a teaspoon of fuel to restart. Idling burns far more over time, and it fouls plugs faster. Turn it off.

**Q: Do “eco” driving modes actually save fuel?**  
A: They do—by making the car feel sluggish. Throttle mapping, shift points, and A/C output are all softened. You’ll save 3–5% if you don’t fight the transmission, but at the cost of drivability.

**Q: Is aerodynamics still relevant with modern SUVs and crossovers?**  
A: Absolutely. Roof racks, bike carriers, and even open windows above 80 km/h create drag that hurts efficiency more than most people realize. Remove what you don’t use.

### 📝 Conclusion

Fuel efficiency isn’t about chasing magic numbers or trusting marketing gimmicks—it’s about consistency, maintenance, and understanding how your vehicle actually works under real-world conditions. You won’t squeeze out extra kilometres from a $50 dongle or by filling with premium unless your engine demands it. What you *will* get is predictable savings from clean filters, proper tire pressure, smooth inputs, and letting modern computers do the heavy lifting where they’re designed to. Drive less when you can, maintain what you have, and stop fighting physics. The road doesn’t care about your dashboard average—it rewards patience, precision, and a little bit of common sense. Keep it simple, keep it maintained, and the numbers will take care of themselves. In the end, efficiency is just good habits wearing a different name.
