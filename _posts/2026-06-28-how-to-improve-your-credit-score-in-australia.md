## 2️⃣ Why Your Score Matters

When you apply for a mortgage or a personal loan, lenders view your **credit report** as the quickest snapshot of your repayment reliability. In Australia, most banks rely on data from **Experian** or Equifax, which range from 0 to 900. Scores above 700 typically unlock lower interest rates and better terms. Below that, you may face higher rates, larger deposit requirements, or outright denial.

The trick isn't just paying bills on time; it's a combination of payment history, **utilisation ratio**, length of credit history, types of credit, and recent **hard inquiry** activity. I've seen clients who were "on the edge" (score 680–690) leap to the "good" zone (>700) by focusing on just two levers: reducing credit card balances and eliminating unnecessary hard inquiries.

## 3️⃣ Mapping Your Credit Health

### 3.1 Get Your Baseline – The Free Annual Report

The first step is knowing where you stand. All three credit bureaus (Experian, Equifax, illion) provide a free annual report via the Australian Credit Reporting Association portal. I recommend ordering all three because each bureau may have slightly different data:

- **Equifax**: Most commonly used by major banks for mortgage assessments.
- **Experian**: Often more transparent about dispute status and retail credit.
- **illion**: Captures a wide array of retail credit and some niche accounts.

Download, print a copy, and highlight any errors or outdated entries. Even a single miss-typed account can drag your score down. ## ❓ Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I need to pay for credit monitoring when Experian and Equifax offer free basic scores?**  
A: Free tiers typically provide a single bureau’s score, limited historical data, and manual dispute steps. Paid services fill those gaps with multi-bureau tracking, automated dispute filing, identity theft alerts, and personalized coaching—especially valuable if you’re rebuilding credit or preparing for a major loan application.

**Q: How often should I check my credit score?**  
A: Once a month is sufficient for most Australians. Frequent checks won’t improve your rating and can lead to unnecessary stress. Focus on consistent payment habits, keeping credit utilization below 30%, and addressing flagged errors promptly.

**Q: Will checking my own credit hurt my score?**  
A: No. Checking your own report or score generates a “soft inquiry,” which never impacts your rating. Only hard inquiries—triggered by lenders during loan, mortgage, or card applications—can cause temporary, minor dips.

**Q: Can I cancel a paid monitoring service at any time?**  
A: Yes, but review the terms carefully. Some services require 30 days’ notice, while others auto-renew unless you opt out through your account settings. Always export your data and set calendar reminders before cancelling to avoid surprise charges or lost insights.
