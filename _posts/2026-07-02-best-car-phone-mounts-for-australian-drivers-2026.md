### Why Australian Conditions Demand Tougher Mounts

* **Legal speed tolerance** – Our maximum legal limits sit between 110–130 km/h on major interstates. A reliable **dashboard mount** must maintain grip under sustained highway speeds and handle the micro-vibrations that degrade cheap adhesives over time.
* **Extreme climate cycling** – From 45°C heat in Outback NSW to humid coastal summers in QLD, plastics become brittle and silicone seals dry out fast. UV resistance and flexible rubber compounds aren’t optional; they’re survival gear.
* **EV adoption & magnetic safety** – As more drivers switch to plug-ins, OEMs are issuing clear warnings about strong neodymium magnets near battery management systems or high-voltage cabling. An **EV-safe mount** must either use low-profile, shielded magnets or rely on mechanical retention.
* **Legal compliance & visibility** – Under the National Vehicle Standards and state road acts, your setup must never obstruct the driver’s visual field, interfere with airbag deployment zones, or block windscreen wipers. Ergonomic angle adjustment isn’t a luxury; it’s a safety requirement.

#### Frequently Asked Questions

**How long does a windscreen suction mount typically last?**  
On average, 18–24 months with proper care. The real enemy isn’t time—it’s UV exposure and repeated thermal cycling. Once you notice the plunger losing its snap or the diaphragm cracking along the edges, it’s time to replace it. Don’t gamble on a failing mount.

**Will extreme heat or cold destroy the suction?**  
Silicone handles temperature swings better than rubber, but prolonged direct sunlight (think 60°C+ windscreen temps) will soften the compound and reduce grip. In summer, park in shade or use a sun visor when possible. In winter, cold makes silicone stiffer, which actually improves initial seal strength, though rapid temperature changes can cause micro-leaks.

**Can I use these on tinted or privacy glass?**  
Only if the tint is factory-applied and fully adhered. Aftermarket window films often lift at the edges under suction pressure, leading to sudden drops. If your windscreen has a rain sensor housing or adhesive bands near the top, avoid mounting there—it compromises both grip and ADAS calibration.

**How do I remove a mount without damaging the glass?**  
Never yank it off. Press the release valve fully, then slide a plastic pry tool or even a credit card under the edge to break the vacuum gradually. Wipe away old adhesive residue with isopropyl alcohol and replace the diaphragm pad when you reinstall.
