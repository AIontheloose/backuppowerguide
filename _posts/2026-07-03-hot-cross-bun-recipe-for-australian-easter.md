### Why We Still Crave the Classic (and How to Make It Uniquely Aussie)

Hot cross buns first appeared in England during the 12th-century religious festivals, where the flour paste cross symbolised Christ’s crucifixion. Over centuries, the treat crossed oceans and landed firmly in Australian Easter celebrations. What I’ve found is that Aussies have always loved to put their own spin on British staples. In 2026, many home bakers are swapping traditional currants for a mix of local macadamia nuts, sultanas, and a gentle drizzle of golden syrup. We’re also leaning into seasonal citrus, folding in a touch of blood orange zest to complement our coastal spring palette. Pair these fresh from the oven with a slice of lemon ricotta cake or pavlova later in the day, and you’ve got a dessert spread that proudly showcases our relaxed, sun-drenched lifestyle. ### Baking Through a Warmer 2026 Spring

The 2026 Easter week has brought unseasonably warm weather to much of New South Wales, Queensland, and Victoria. Higher ambient temperatures mean your dough will proof noticeably faster, but it also demands slightly more care to keep the crumb moist and prevent over-fermentation. I recommend adding a teaspoon of finely grated lemon zest to the dry mix; the citrus oils help retain moisture while cutting through the richness of the butter. Keep an eye on your kitchen’s ambient temperature during proofing—if it climbs above 35°C, move the bowl to a cooler bench or slightly shorten the first rise by 10 minutes. I aim for a dough temperature of around 24–26°C before the initial bulk fermentation. Gentle pacing always wins over rushing in warm kitchens.

### The Recipe: Golden Macadamia & Golden Syrup Hot Cross Buns

#### Ingredients
**For the dough:**
- 500 g plain flour, plus extra for dusting
- 60 g caster sugar
- 1 tsp fine sea salt
- 2 tsp ground cinnamon
- ½ tsp freshly grated nutmeg
- ¼ tsp ground cloves
- 7 g instant yeast
- 2 large eggs, lightly beaten
- 250 ml full cream milk, warmed to lukewarm
- 60 g unsalted butter, softened
- 1 tbsp golden syrup, plus extra for brushing

**For the filling & glaze:**
- 150 g mixed dried fruit (sultanas, raisins, diced apple)
- 60 g macadamia nuts, roughly chopped
- **Flour paste crosses:** 3 tbsp plain flour mixed with 2–3 tbsp water until smooth

#### Method

**Step 1: Activate and Mix** `[Photo caption: A warm bowl of milk and yeast bubbling gently on a sunlit kitchen bench, ready to meet the flour.]`
In a large mixing bowl, combine the lukewarm milk, beaten eggs, golden syrup, and instant yeast. Give it a quick whisk and let it sit for 5 minutes until foamy. In a separate bowl, sift together the flour, sugar, salt, cinnamon, nutmeg, and cloves. Make a well in the centre and pour in the wet mixture along with the softened butter.

**Step 2: Knead to Perfect Tension**
Using a wooden spoon or your hands, bring the dough together until it forms a shaggy mass. Turn it onto a lightly floured benchtop and knead for 8–10 minutes. The dough should feel soft, slightly tacky, but clean off the sides of your bowl. This is the perfect time to get the kids involved—kneading builds arm strength and turns baking into active play.

**Step 3: Fold in the Fillings**
Once smooth, press the mixed fruit and macadamias evenly into the dough. Avoid overworking at this stage, as it can make the buns tough. Shape the dough into a ball, place it back in the bowl, and cover with a damp tea towel or cling wrap. `[Photo caption: Hands gently pressing golden syrup into the scored tops before the final proof.]`

**Step 4: First Proof (Bulk Fermentation)**
Leave to rise in a warm, draft-free spot for 50–60 minutes. In our warmer 2026 spring kitchens, this may only take 40 minutes. The dough is ready when it has doubled in size and springs back slowly when poked with a floured finger.

**Step 5: Shape and Second Proof**
Punch down the dough gently to release excess gas. Divide into 12 equal portions (about 70 g each) and shape into tight rounds by tucking the edges underneath. Place on a lined baking tray, leaving 3 cm between each bun. Cover and prove for 25–30 minutes until puffy and nearly doubled again.

**Step 6: Score, Bake, and Glaze**
Preheat your oven to 180°C fan-forced. Pipe or spread the flour paste crosses onto each bun. Bake for 20–25 minutes until golden brown and sounding hollow when tapped on the base. Immediately brush with a warm mixture of 2 tbsp golden syrup and 1 tbsp hot water. The sugar glaze will set into a beautiful, glossy finish as they cool.

Let them rest on a wire rack for 10 minutes before wrapping in foil or placing in an airtight container. ### Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I freeze the dough before or after baking?**
Yes, you have two excellent options depending on your schedule. You can freeze the shaped buns raw on the tray for up to a month, then thaw them overnight in the fridge before baking as usual. Alternatively, bake the full batch, let them cool completely, and store them in a freezer-safe container for up to three months. When you’re ready to eat, simply wrap them in foil and warm them at 150°C for 8–10 minutes, or toast individual slices with a knob of butter for a proper spring morning treat.

**What should I do if the dough feels too sticky to handle?**
Warm kitchens often encourage rapid hydration, which can make dough feel wetter than expected. Rather than adding heaps of extra flour, try chilling your benchtop and hands in cold water before kneading. You can also rest the dough for 10 minutes; this allows the gluten to relax and the flour to fully absorb the liquid, naturally firming up the texture. If it’s still tacky after resting, add flour one tablespoon at a time until it clears the sides of the bowl but remains soft enough to stretch.

**Can I make these buns dairy-free or vegan-friendly?**
Absolutely. Swap the butter for a neutral-flavoured coconut oil or plant-based baking spread, and use a flax egg (1 tbsp ground flax mixed with 3 tbsp water) in place of the beaten egg. For the milk, oat or soy milk works beautifully and holds moisture well during proofing. The golden syrup is naturally vegan, but double-check your dried fruit blend to ensure it wasn’t processed with animal-derived glycerine. These adaptations keep the crumb tender and the flavour profile just as comforting for the whole family.

**Is flour paste better than icing sugar for the crosses?**
Flour paste remains the traditional Aussie method and is generally superior for texture and authenticity. Icing sugar melts into a hard, sugary crust that can crack when you bite through the soft bun, whereas flour paste bakes into a tender, slightly chewy line that complements the spiced crumb perfectly. It also avoids the common issue of icing sliding off during proofing or baking. If you do prefer a sweeter finish, pipe the flour crosses lightly and dust them with caster sugar after glazing for a subtle sparkle without the hardness.
