### Grounding the Conversation in Local Reality

Before we dive into product shelves, it's essential to acknowledge what makes our outdoor spaces uniquely demanding. Australian conditions aren't just "hot"; they're a cocktail of stressors that standard coatings simply can't handle.

*   **Extreme UV and thermal cycling:** Much of Australia experiences over 2,500 hours of annual sunlight. Temperature swings exceeding 30°C between day and night cause timber to expand and contract rapidly. This movement cracks brittle coatings within months if the paint lacks flexibility.
*   **Coastal salt spray and humidity:** For anyone living within 10km of the coastline, airborne chloride accelerates wood rot and paint blistering. A standard **coastal fence coating** must have superior adhesion and moisture repellency to stand a chance.
*   **Regulatory shifts on VOCs:** Since the tightening of standards in mid-2024, Australian regulations have significantly restricted volatile organic compounds (VOCs). This means oil-heavy paints are being phased out in favour of advanced waterborne acrylics and elastomeric blends. These new formulations perform better, dry faster, and are safer for our environment while meeting strict **VOC‑compliant outdoor paint** requirements.
*   **Material diversity dictates chemistry:** Most Aussie homes use treated pine palings, seasoned hardwood slats, or Colourbond steel. Each absorbs moisture differently. Treated pine is porous and alkaline; hardwoods are dense and oily; metal expands with heat. Your primer and topcoat must be compatible with your specific substrate.

3. **pH neutrality wins:** Timber prep matters more than paint brand. A simple vinegar rinse (1:4 with water) neutralises alkaline residues from pressure treatment, ensuring better adhesion. Skip it, and you’ll face peeling within 18 months.
4. **UV protection isn’t optional in Australia:** Coastal sun degrades binders faster than salt. Look for coatings rated ≥300 g/m² UV absorbers. Resene’s Low Sheen Woodguard or Dulux Weathershield both clock in around AUD $65/L but last 6–7 years longer than standard glosses.

### Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I use interior paint on an outdoor fence?**  
A: Absolutely not. Interior formulations lack UV stabilisers, flex agents, and mildewcides required for exterior exposure. Within a single summer, you’ll see yellowing, cracking, and rapid salt degradation. Always choose a certified exterior-grade coating.

**Q: How do I know if my fence needs sandblasting or just washing?**  
A: If the existing paint is still adhering firmly with no flaking or rust bleed, high-pressure washing (1500–2000 PSI) with a timber-safe cleaner is sufficient. Sandblasting or chemical stripping is only necessary when coatings have delaminated or salt corrosion has penetrated the wood grain.

**Q: Is it worth buying expensive “coastal-grade” paint in inland areas?**  
A: Not necessarily. If you’re 50 km+ from the coast, a standard acrylic latex fence paint (AUD $40–$50/L) performs identically to premium marine formulations. Save your budget for thicker application coats and proper surface prep instead.

**Q: Can I apply new paint over old gloss without sanding?**  
A: Glossy surfaces repel fresh coatings. Light scuff-sanding with 80-grit paper or using a liquid deglosser creates the mechanical key needed for adhesion. Skipping this step guarantees peeling within two years, regardless of paint quality.

**Q: How often should I reapply salt-tolerant fence paint?**  
A: In direct coastal spray zones, plan on recoating every 4–5 years with a quality acrylic or epoxy-modified system. Inland or sheltered locations can extend this to 6–8 years. Keep a maintenance log and track colour fading or chalkiness as your true indicators.
