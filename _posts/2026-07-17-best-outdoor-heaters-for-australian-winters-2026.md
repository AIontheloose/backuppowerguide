## Choosing Your Heat Source: A Practical Breakdown

Before we jump into specific models, let's talk about what you genuinely need. Australian winters vary wildly, from the damp, bone-chilling cold of Victoria to the crisp, dry chill of Western Australia. Understanding how each heater type performs will help you avoid buyer's remorse and get the most bang for your buck.

### Electric Patio Heaters: The Crowd-Pleasers
Electric patio heaters remain a top choice for good reason. They're clean, quiet, and incredibly straightforward to set up—just plug in and enjoy. In 2026, standard portable units typically range from **$200 to $350 AUD**, making them highly accessible for DIYers on a budget. I love these because they require no gas bottles, complex wiring, or ongoing fuel refills. However, keep in mind that most freestanding models effectively cover about **1.2 m²** of seated area, which is perfect for intimate conversation pits but may struggle to warm a large lawn.

**Pro Tip:** If you're watching your power bills, look for models with variable wattage settings (usually 1 kW to 3 kW). You rarely need full blast on mild winter evenings; a gentle 1.5 kW setting often provides just enough warmth while keeping running costs around $0.40–$0.60 per hour, depending on your state's electricity rate.

### Gas-Powered Powerhouses
When the wind whips across the coast and electric heaters feel like they're fighting a losing battle, gas steps in as the reliable workhorse. A propane gas heater delivers significantly higher raw heat output, making it ideal for large open decks or exposed coastal areas. Current pricing sits around **$400 to $520 AUD** for quality units. While the upfront cost is steeper, the raw power justifies it if you prioritise speed and intensity over long-term running costs.

> **Pro Tip:** Gas heaters deliver intense warmth quickly, but always check your local council regulations and strata rules before installation. Many apartment blocks restrict high-output appliances or open-flame risks in dense housing areas. Factor in ongoing propane bottle costs (roughly $35–$45 per refill) when calculating your winter budget.

### Infrared Heaters: Efficiency Kings
Here's a personal favourite for the budget-aware gardener: infrared heaters. Unlike traditional electric models that warm the air, infrared technology targets objects and people directly. This means you feel the warmth instantly, even on breezy nights. The **Honeywell IR300** (or equivalent 2026 models) typically retails around **$310 AUD**. Best of all? They consume roughly **30% less energy** than standard convective electric heaters while delivering focused, directional warmth. Over a typical winter, this efficiency can save you $50–$70 in running costs compared to older portable units.

### Solar & Eco-Friendly Options
For the eco-conscious home renovator, solar is gaining serious traction. A quality **solar-powered patio heater** usually costs between **$140 and $165 AUD**. These units rely on integrated panels that charge during daylight hours, providing warmth as the sun sets. While they won't match gas power in extreme cold, their running costs are virtually zero once installed. For gardens receiving 5–6 peak sunlight hours daily, you can realistically save around $40–$60 annually compared to grid-powered electric units. They're particularly brilliant for sunny spots where panels can charge uninterrupted.

## Placement, Safety & Maintenance Checklist

Getting the most out of your heater isn't just about buying the right unit; it's about placing and maintaining it correctly. Here's a quick step-by-step checklist to keep your setup safe and efficient:

* **Wind & Placement:** Position your heater upwind from seating areas so the warmth blows toward you, not away. Keep units at least 2 metres from walls or flammable materials, and ensure freestanding models sit on level ground with a stable base. For gas units, never place them under enclosed pergolas or in poorly ventilated alcoves.
* **Strata & Council Rules:** If you live in an apartment complex or managed community, check your building's bylaws before purchasing high-output heaters. Many strata schemes restrict gas appliances or mandate specific clearance distances. Local councils may also have fire risk guidelines for open-flame devices during dry summer-to-winter transition months.
* **Routine Maintenance:** Wipe down electric grilles monthly with a dry cloth to prevent dust buildup. For gas heaters, inspect hoses and regulators yearly for cracks or wear, and replace batteries in ignition units promptly. Cover all units when not in use for extended periods to protect against rain and salt air corrosion.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**1. Which outdoor heater is best for small balconies in Australian apartments?**
I highly recommend a compact infrared or low-wattage electric model for balcony living. These units deliver targeted warmth without overwhelming the space, and they draw minimal power from standard outlets. Always verify your building's fire safety regulations first, as many apartment complexes restrict gas appliances or require specific clearance distances from balustrades and shared walls.

**2. How do I calculate the running costs of an electric patio heater?**
You can estimate running costs by multiplying the heater's wattage by your local electricity rate per kilowatt-hour. For example, a 2 kW unit running for three hours daily at $0.45/kWh will cost roughly $2.70 per day. Opting for variable wattage settings and using the heater only during peak chill hours can significantly reduce your winter energy bills while still keeping you comfortable.

**3. Do I need to store my outdoor heater over summer?**
Yes, storing or properly covering your heater is essential for longevity. Australian summers bring intense UV exposure, heavy rains, and salt air that can degrade plastics, corrode metal joints, and damage electrical components. If you live in a coastal region, consider investing in a breathable, waterproof cover designed specifically for outdoor heating equipment to prevent moisture trapping and mould growth.

**4. What wattage should I look for based on my outdoor space size?**
Match your wattage to the area you actually need to warm. For intimate seating covering up to 2 m², a 1–1.5 kW heater is sufficient. Medium decks (3–5 m²) benefit from 2–2.5 kW models, while large open entertaining zones may require 3 kW+ units or supplemental gas heaters. Remember that infrared heaters feel warmer at lower wattages because they target surfaces directly, whereas convective electric models warm the entire air mass around you.
