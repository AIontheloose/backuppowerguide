### Step-by-Step Tiling Process

#### Prepping the Wall and Applying Levelling Compound
Start by clearing your benchtop and removing any old splashback material, whether that’s laminate, glass, or loose tiles. Clean the wall thoroughly with a degreaser to remove cooking oils and dust, then tap it lightly with a hammer handle. Hollow sounds indicate delaminated plaster that must be scraped back to a solid substrate. Once the surface is clean, dry, and structurally sound, mix your wall levelling compound according to the manufacturer’s ratio. Use a trowel to apply a 3–5 mm coat, focusing on dips or bulges. Let it cure for 24 hours. Skipping this step is the number one reason DIY splashbacks fail prematurely, as uneven walls force tiles to rock and crack over time.

> **Pro Tip:** Always check your wall with a spirit level before applying screed. If your wall deviates by more than 3 mm across a one-metre span, the compound will save you from fighting crooked tiles later. SmoothLay works wonders here because it self-compacts as it dries, giving you a truly flat plane to work with.

#### Mixing Mortar and Laying Tiles
Open your thin-set mortar and mix it to a peanut-butter consistency. Let it slake (rest) for ten minutes, then stir again to reactivate the polymers. Using a notched trowel technique, comb the adhesive onto a one-metre square section of wall. Press the tile firmly into place with a slight twisting motion to ensure proper tile adhesive bonding across the entire back surface. Insert plastic cross spacers at each corner to maintain consistent grout lines. Work from the bottom up, checking your level every third tile. #### Cutting Around Outlets and Corners
Electrical sockets, power points, and cabinetry edges are where DIY projects usually go off track. Mark your tile with a pencil, measure twice, and score gently with a manual tile cutter or use your electric saw for thicker porcelain. Always prioritise silica dust safety by wearing a P2 respirator and goggles when cutting—silica particles are invisible but harmful if inhaled. For internal corners, use pre-mitred corner tiles or cut two pieces at 45 degrees to meet flush. Leave a consistent 5 mm expansion joints at the floor line and where the splashback meets cabinetry; this allows for natural thermal movement without cracking your grout or tiles.

#### Grouting and Finishing
Once all tiles have cured (usually 24 hours), mix your epoxy grout according to package instructions. Epoxy is thicker and faster-setting than cement-based alternatives, so work in manageable 0.5 m² sections during your epoxy grout application process. Press the grout into joints with a rubber float held at a 45-degree angle, then wipe away excess with a damp sponge before it skins over. After thirty minutes, polish the tiles with a dry microfiber cloth to remove any haze. Allow 72 hours of curing before exposing the splashback to steam or splashes.

> **Pro Tip:** Epoxy grout cures quickly and bonds aggressively. If you miss a spot during wiping, don’t scrub hard—wait an hour for it to soften slightly, then gently buff it away. Rushing this stage is what causes cloudy finishes and uneven joints.

### FAQ

**Q: Can I skip wall leveling if my plaster looks flat?**  
A: Visually flat doesn’t mean structurally flat. A 2-metre straight edge will reveal dips or humps exceeding the 3 mm tolerance most tile manufacturers require. Skipping levelling guarantees uneven grout lines, trapped moisture, and premature failure.

**Q: What’s the difference between regular cement mortar and polymer-modified thin-set?**  
A: Polymer-modified thin-set contains latex or acrylic additives that improve flexibility, bond strength, and water/heat resistance. For kitchen backsplashes and floors exposed to thermal cycling, it’s non-negotiable. Regular cement mortar is only suitable for dry, low-stress interior walls.

**Q: How do I calculate expansion joints for a large kitchen?**  
A: Install perimeter expansion joints (5 mm) where tile meets cabinets, walls, or thresholds. For long continuous runs over 3 metres, add intermediate movement joints filled with flexible silicone. Always follow your tile and adhesive manufacturer’s specifications.

**Q: Is it worth hiring a licensed tiler in Australia?**  
A: If you’re dealing with substrate repairs, waterproofing, large-format tiles, or complex cuts, yes. DIY errors often cost 2–3× more in rectification than professional installation. Always verify your tiler holds a current licence and carries insurance.
