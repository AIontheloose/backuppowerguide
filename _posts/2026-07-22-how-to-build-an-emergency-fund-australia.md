### Defining Your Target: The Numbers Behind the Safety Net

The first step is determining exactly how much cash you need. General advice suggests three to six months of living expenses, but for a typical four-person Australian household spending $7,200 per month, this translates to a target range of **$21,600–$43,200 AUD**.

Static targets are dangerous in an inflationary environment. If we assume core inflation persists above the RBA’s 2–3% target band due to persistent supply chain pressures and building cost indices, the real value of a fixed dollar amount erodes annually. I recommend calculating your target based on *current* monthly outflows, then applying an annual uplift factor to your goal until you reach it. This ensures your fund doesn’t become insufficient simply because you took too long to build it.

Context matters significantly. With the median house price in Sydney hovering around AUD 1.2 million, many homeowners are servicing debt that can easily exceed their monthly savings rate. If your mortgage interest outflow is $600 per month while you’re only saving $200, you need a larger buffer to survive income shocks. **The After-Tax Yield Reality**
Interest earned on savings accounts, term deposits, and money market funds is fully taxable at your marginal tax rate. For a taxpayer in the 32.5% bracket, a gross yield of 3.00% drops to an after-tax yield of approximately 2.02%. High-income earners (37% bracket) see further compression to 1.89%. Consequently, when evaluating a cash reserve, you must compare the net return against your actual cost of capital and inflation expectations. An offset account often provides superior risk-adjusted returns for mortgagors because interest savings are tax-free, effectively delivering a gross yield equal to your mortgage rate.

### Frequently Asked Questions

**How much should I prioritise in my emergency fund before investing?**
You should establish a fully funded liquidity buffer covering three to six months of essential expenses before allocating surplus capital to growth assets. This foundational step prevents you from being forced to liquidate investments at market lows during unexpected cash flow shortfalls, thereby protecting compounding returns over time.

**Is a money market fund safer than a high-interest savings account?**
Both instruments carry low risk, but they operate under different regulatory frameworks. High-interest savings accounts are covered by the Australian Government’s Financial Claims Scheme up to $250,000 per institution, whereas money market funds are not deposit-guaranteed and fluctuate daily with short-term interest rates. For absolute capital preservation, an HFSA remains preferable; for slightly higher after-tax yields, a money market fund may align better with your risk tolerance.

**Does the tax impact change how I should structure my emergency savings?**
Yes, taxation significantly influences net returns and vehicle selection. Interest income is added to your assessable income and taxed at your marginal rate, which compresses real gains during high-inflation periods. Utilising an offset account effectively delivers tax-free interest savings, making it mathematically superior for mortgagors, while non-mortgage households may benefit from diversifying across term deposits and low-fee ETFs to optimise after-tax yield.

**How often should I rebalance or adjust my emergency fund target?**
You should review your target annually using the inflation uplift method, but adjust contribution levels quarterly based on salary changes, interest rate movements, and household shifts. If your risk profile changes due to job stability or dependent care requirements, modify the buffer size immediately rather than waiting for a calendar cycle to ensure adequate financial resilience.
