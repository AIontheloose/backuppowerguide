G'day everyone, it's Emma Torres here. When I first started supporting participants through their NDIS journeys years ago, I was struck by how many focused almost exclusively on mobility aids and transport. While those are absolutely vital, I've seen time and again that stretching your plan across fitness, nutrition, and mental well-being creates a ripple effect of independence that changes lives.

That's a significant chunk of funding that can be strategically repurposed to build your strength, energy, and resilience. ### Understanding 'Reasonable and Necessary' in 2026

The NDIS funds supports that are "reasonable and necessary" to help you achieve your goals. However, a crucial legal caveat to remember: this test is interpreted differently by various planners and the NDIA. One planner might approve a service that another declines based on local guidelines.

**Legal & Practical Advice:**
*   **Keep your plan reference number handy.** If a claim is rejected, ask for a written statement explaining the decision so you can appeal or provide further evidence.
*   **Link everything to a goal.** Funding is rarely approved without a clear connection to a specific outcome in your plan, such as "improved physical function" or "increased social participation."

### Evidence Collection Checklist

To ensure your claims for nutrition and fitness are approved, gather the following documents before submitting your plan amendment:

1.  **Medical Necessity Letter:** From your GP or specialist detailing your condition (e.g., spinal cord injury) and how the support addresses a specific health risk.
2.  **Functional Capacity Assessment:** A recent report outlining how fitness or nutrition interventions will improve your daily function.
3.  **Quotes & Invoices:** Itemised quotes for equipment, specifying that cheaper alternatives are unsuitable for your disability.
4.  **Lab Results:** If claiming protein supplements or specific diets, include blood work showing deficiencies (e.g., low vitamin D, albumin levels).

**Storage Tip:** Keep a digital folder

...of all your evidence, organized by category and date. Naming files clearly (e.g., `2023-10_MedicalLetter_GP.pdf`) helps NDIA planners locate documents quickly during review, reducing the chances of delays due to missing information.

### Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: Can I claim a standard gym membership through the NDIS?**
A: Generally, no. The NDIS does not fund everyday costs like general leisure activities. However, you may be eligible for funding for specific *assistive equipment* or *therapeutic exercise programs* if they are directly linked to your disability and improve your functional capacity. You must demonstrate that the gym environment or equipment is necessary for rehabilitation or managing your condition, rather than just fitness for fitness' sake.

**Q: Will the NDIS pay for protein supplements or special diets?**
A: This depends on medical necessity. Standard food costs are usually considered everyday living expenses. Specialized nutritional supplements may be approved if you have a documented inability to meet nutrient requirements through regular food due to your disability (e.g., swallowing difficulties, metabolic conditions). You will need strong clinical evidence from a dietitian or GP proving that standard dietary support is insufficient.

**Q: What should I do if my plan amendment is rejected?**
A: First, request detailed feedback from the NDIA on why it was declined. Often, rejections are due to insufficient documentation rather than invalid needs. Consult your support coordinator or allied health professional to strengthen your evidence—perhaps by obtaining a more specific Functional Capacity Assessment or clarifying the link between the funding and your disability goals. You also have the right to request an internal review or apply to the Administrative Appeals Tribunal (AAT) if necessary.
