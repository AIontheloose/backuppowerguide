Portable Generators for Home Backup Power – How to Choose the Right One

Choosing a portable generator for your home can feel overwhelming, but by breaking down the key factors—fuel type, power capacity, features, and safety—you can find a unit that balances reliability, cost, and ease of use.

Calculate Your Power Needs

Start by listing essential appliances: refrigerator, lights, small HVAC, and any medical devices. Sum their continuous wattage and add a surge factor for devices that need a brief power spike (e.g., refrigerators, air conditioners). For most households, a 5,000‑to‑7,000‑watt generator covers the basics, while 8,000–10,000 watts handles more demanding setups.

Fuel Choices and Their Trade‑Offs

Gasoline generators are light and inexpensive but require frequent refueling and produce more emissions. Diesel models are more fuel‑efficient and durable, ideal for heavy usage, but they’re bulkier. Propane and natural gas generators run cleaner, can be linked to home gas lines for seamless refueling, and are quieter, though they often cost more upfront.

Key Features for Home Backup

Look for a generator with a built‑in transfer switch or automatic transfer system, so you can seamlessly switch from grid to generator power. A low‑oil alert, electric start, and quiet‑operation mode help keep you comfortable and safe. A rear or side access panel simplifies maintenance and refueling.

Safety First: Placement and Compliance

Never run a generator inside a building or closed space; always place it at least 20 feet from windows, doors, and vents. Keep it outdoors, with a carbon‑monoxide detector nearby. Follow local noise and emission regulations, and install an automatic shut‑off to prevent over‑loading.
